Support plans

Part of the assessment process is writing up support plans. Support plans need to accurately capture what the goals of the service user are, explain how support services can help them achieve their goals, and what strategies and resources will be the most useful. A support plan should include: information about the service user, what their goals are, what strategies to use, what resources to access, and later on, what the outcomes were, how progress was monitored, and a review of how effective the plan was.

Download the Support Plan Template and follow the example below. Read the information in the background tab first and consider how you might fill in the other headings before opening the tabs for example responses.

Select each of the headings to learn what needs to be included in a support plan. When you have finished, select again to close the section.

Background

Information about the service user:

Lucy is a big fan of ice hockey and would like to be more involved in the sport. Her disability prevents her from playing ice hockey, but she enjoys watching games and reading about the sport. Tickets to national league games are quite expensive so she can’t watch live games very often. When she does go, she loves the energy and excitement of the crowds.

Goals

What does the service user want to achieve?

Lucy wants to be more actively involved in ice hockey.

Strategies

What practical steps can we take?

Contact accessible ice hockey arenas and recreational ice hockey leagues to see if there are any volunteer opportunities that would be suitable for Lucy.

Resources

Who could we contact or what programs could the service user enrol and participate in?

Contact Ice Hockey Victoria and ice hockey rinks, such as the O’Brien Group Arena and the Olympic Ice Skating Centre.

Outcomes

What were the results from applying the strategies?

Lucy was welcome to volunteer at recreational ice hockey games at the different arenas. They usually had trouble finding people to man the penalty boxes, especially for the leagues with older players. Lucy was a great and consistent help. She met and befriended other fans, including those who played and those who loved to watch the game. In addition to manning the penalty box, Lucy got to learn how to keep score and update the scoreboard during games. She got to know the arena staff and see how they prepared and maintained the ice.

Lucy was thrilled when she was invited to ride on a Zamboni to resurface the ice. From volunteering with recreational leagues, Lucy met people involved with the national ice hockey league. She now volunteers at national league games too, helping to scan tickets and roll out the red carpet on the ice for the national anthem. Lucy feels she is a member of the ice hockey community now and has made many friends who share her passion for the sport.

Monitoring

How was the progress of the strategy checked?

Regular meetings with Lucy were held where we talked about the volunteer work, the people she met and interacted with, and how she felt it was going. There were also regular conversations with Lucy’s family, with the organisers, and with people Lucy worked with at the arenas where she volunteered.

These discussions were to make sure there weren’t any difficulties for Lucy getting to and from games, that her time spent volunteering wasn’t negatively impacting other areas in her life, that she was successfully completing her assigned volunteer tasks, and that everyone involved was having a positive experience.

Review

How effective were the strategies to help the service user achieve their goals?

Contacting the recreational ice hockey leagues for volunteering opportunities was a great start to getting Lucy more actively involved in the sport. She feels that she is now part of the ice hockey community as opposed to watching from the sidelines. She has made many new friends and has been able to experience things she had only read about previously (like riding a Zamboni).

The community she has joined has been very encouraging, while also challenging her as well (e.g. teaching her how to operate the scoreboard). It has also given Lucy greater independence as she now commutes to games with ice hockey friends instead of relying on her family to drive her.
