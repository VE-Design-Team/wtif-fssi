Assessment processes

The purpose of an assessment process is to define the goals of a person with disability and how they can best achieve them. While there may be many smaller goals that the service user is aiming for, they all feed into the overarching goal of greater control and independence in their life. We can support this by helping them form meaningful relationships with people in their wider community who are outside of their service providers. Setting goals and finding the best ways for a service user to reach them begins with assessment processes. These processes should be person-centred and strength-based.

Person-centred planning

Person-centred planning involves working with the person, not planning for them. Planning should include the service user and everyone who is important to them. This includes their family and carers, support networks and services, and health professionals. Listening is key to person-centred planning.

The aim is to learn about who the person is beyond their disability, what is important to them, and what their aspirations are. Building a good relationship where you get to know the individual is important to helping them create personalised, realistic and achievable goals.

Strength-based approach

A strength-based approach emphasises the person’s strongest capabilities instead of focusing on immediate problems and solutions. The aim is to identify the person’s strengths and develop strategies around them. It is about what the individual can do and how they want to do things.

Like person-centred planning, the strength-based approach involves meeting with the service user and the people they are closest to. To get a full picture of the person’s capabilities, you need to consider:

  • What can the person do now on their own?
  • What can the person do with support?
  • What can the person do in the future?
