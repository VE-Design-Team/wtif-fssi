Getting feedback

Feedback is a necessary part of knowing whether or not you are successfully meeting your work responsibilities, identifying areas for further development, and learning how you can improve your work.

You can get feedback from the service user, their carers and family, your work colleagues, and your supervisor. It can be formal (e.g. a performance review) or informal (e.g. comments or suggestions that come up during the day or a situation).

How you receive feedback will affect your future performance and relationships with others in the workplace. By reacting and responding to others and their feedback in a polite and considerate way, they will treat you likewise.

React

Be aware of your response. How are you taking it? Are your emotions affecting your ability to pay attention to what is being said? Listen. Don’t interrupt and try to explain yourself before hearing everything they have to say. You will have an opportunity to respond, but ensure that you have all the information first.

Reflect

Be open to feedback. If you are feeling especially upset or angry, take a mental step back. This is about the work, about how to improve support for the service user, and not about you personally. Make sure you fully understand what is being said. Ask for specific examples of when, where, and how you could do things differently for better outcomes.

Respond

Once the person has given you all of their feedback, accept it and thank them for it. If you disagree with what they’ve said, politely explain why and provide facts for your reasoning. The aim is to agree on options and solutions for the future that will improve the care of service users.

Follow-up

If you are trying out different methods of addressing a situation, have a follow-up conversation about how it went. Don’t feel that you must wait for someone to offer you feedback. It is good to ask, especially if you are unsure or unclear about anything. Ask ‘how’ and ‘why’ questions to get detailed explanations as opposed to simple ‘yes or no’ answers. Give the other person time to think about your questions so they can provide you with proper constructive feedback.
