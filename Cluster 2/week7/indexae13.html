The 5 levels of support

Support workers should provide just enough support to encourage a person with a disability to do as much for themselves as possible. Although it may be quicker for you to perform the task for them, it is better for the person with a disability to be as self-sufficient as possible.

There are 5 levels of support that you can provide: prompt, instruct, demonstrate, guide, and complete. The first four levels are for supporting service users with tasks that they will be able to eventually complete on their own. The last level of support is for individuals who are unable to carry out certain tasks because their disability limits their ability to do so.

 Depending on the needs of the person with disability, you can modify the level of support you provide. As they become adept and efficient at a task, you can reduce the amount of support you provide.

Prompt

The first level of support is prompting the service user to begin or carry out a task. For example, when a service user is getting ready for the day in the bathroom, you can prompt them by asking open-ended questions, like ‘What needs to be done next?’. Open-ended questions will encourage the service user to think about what tasks they need to get done (e.g. brushing their teeth, flossing, brushing their hair, etc.). By having to think, it may help them establish a routine on their own with time.

Avoid asking leading questions, such as ‘Isn’t it time to brush your teeth?’. These types of questions can be patronising and unhelpful for the service user to develop greater independence. They may think it is your responsibility to remember when they need to brush their teeth instead of having to think about it on their own.

Instruct

The second level of support is providing instructions to the service user. This involves explaining, one step at a time, how to do something by giving both verbal and visual instructions. For example, when instructing a person on how to brush their teeth, you could tell them to hold their toothbrush at a 45-degree angle to their gums while using your fingers to show them what a 45-degree angle is.

Follow this with an explanation about brushing back and forth with short strokes, and to brush the outer, inner, and chewing surfaces of their teeth. Be sure to check that they understand and are able to follow your instructions properly.

Demonstrate

The third level of support is to demonstrate to the service user how to complete a task. This means showing the service user how to do the task step-by-step. They can either follow-along or do the task immediately after your demonstration. For example, you can demonstrate by brushing your own teeth. The service user could copy your movements at the same time or finish watching you first before brushing their own teeth.

Guide

The fourth level of support is guiding the service user through a task. Guidance involves giving direct physical assistance. For example, you could place your hand over the service user’s hand to direct their movements for brushing their teeth while explaining the steps.

This would give them visual, verbal, and physical cues for how to hold their toothbrush, the type of brushstrokes to make, and what parts of the mouth to brush. You should not be holding their hand the whole time. Guide them at the start, let go, and have them continue on their own. Only step in again if they are having difficulties and need extra support.

Complete

The last level is providing complete support for the person with a disability. This should only be done if all previous support options either haven’t worked or aren’t available. For example, a person with cerebral palsy may understand why it is important to brush one’s teeth twice a day, but their disability makes it impossible for them to do it themselves. They would be reliant on a carer or support worker to carry out this task for them.
