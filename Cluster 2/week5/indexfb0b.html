Environmental and systems improvements

Environments and systems can enhance or diminish a person’s disability. They can be enabling, lessening the impact of a disability by providing the person equal access to resources and services; or disabling by highlighting the person’s disability and restricting what they can do. Environmental factors include the natural (e.g. weather, terrain) and built (e.g. computer software, aid equipment) environments, while systems refer to social, political and economic systems.

The environment and systems are intertwined. How society views and defines disability influences laws and policies and directs public spending towards creating accessible environments. There are three attributes to consider when modifying environmental factors to support people with disabilities:

  1. Object availability: a service user can reach an object on their own or with little help needed.
  2. Accessibility: a service user can go where they want, retrieve information they need, or operate a device with minimal assistance.
  3. Sensory stimulation: having visual, auditory or tactile options to interpret their surroundings.
Practice identifying the correct attribute being addressed for each environmental factor that has been modified to support people with disability.
