Nutrition

Almost seventy per cent of people with a severe disability are overweight or obese, with close to twenty per cent having to manage diabetes in their mid-20s. Poor nutrition leads to excessive weight gain, making it hard to be physically active and the development of chronic health problems like diabetes, heart disease, depression, osteoarthritis, etc. You can help service users develop healthier eating habits through education by encouraging greater independence and working together to create realistic support plans.

This involves teaching them how to read and interpret nutritional information on food packaging and ingredients lists, and finding ways for them to take a more active role in the decisions about how their food is selected and prepared. There are many online resources for nutritional information, healthy meal planning, and meal preparation and recipes. Some examples are Eat for Health, The Healthy Weight Guide, The Dietician’s Association of Australia and The Eat Well Be Active Nutrition Kit.

Select each of the cards to flip and reveal more about the difficulties people with disability face in terms of nutrition.

Lack of Choice

In community residences, occupants may have little-to-no say over what food is being served. The meals being provided may follow outdated nutritional standards or the cooking staff may lack nutritional awareness and do not have the knowledge or skills to create healthier meals.

Reliance on Personal Support Aids

People with disability may rely on personal support aids to prepare their meals. The aid may only be there at certain times of the day that don’t align with the service user’s meal times. They may be unable to go to the grocery store to pick up ingredients and opt to prepare something for the service user that is quick and convenient (e.g. microwavable meal) so they can attend to other responsibilities.

Medications

People with disabilities may have to take medication that has weight gain as a side effect.

Disability Limitations

The person’s disability may make it more difficult to select and prepare meals. For example, a person might have trouble chewing and swallowing harder foods as part of their disability.

Mobility

People with disabilities may have difficulties getting to and around grocery stores. Inaccessible stores with narrow aisles, products out of reach on high shelves, and crowds can be big deterrents for service users. They may decide to only buy microwavable meals or tinned soup to minimise their time in the store or choose to eat out or order in because it is more convenient.

Misconception that Healthier Food is More Expensive

This is the mistaken belief that healthy food is only fresh food and that it is more expensive. This is untrue, as tinned and frozen food can also be very healthy options. Knowing what to look for in ingredients lists and nutritional information on food packaging will tell you what the best options are.
