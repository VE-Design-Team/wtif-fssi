Independence and interdependence

Independence for people with a disability is the desire to have access to the same opportunities as everyone else. This includes having choice and control over their own lives.

It is important to recognise that no one is completely independent. We are all dependent on each other, from school children to the prime minister of Australia. We are all interdependent. Interdependence is a two-way relationship.

Independence is best achieved in partnership with others; through interdependence.

For each of the following cards, decide whether the statement is a definition of independence or interdependence. When you are done, select each card to flip and reveal the answer.

Recognising when you may need help and asking for it.

Independence

Being independent does not mean having to do everything on your own. Knowing when and how you might need assistance is part of independence.

Leads to greater knowledge, resources, and opportunities.

Interdependence

Interdependence involves sharing what we know and have and our experiences. This exposes us to new information, different perspectives, greater understandings, and opportunities for growth.

Having autonomy.

Independence

Autonomy is having the freedom to decide how you want to live. This means being an active participant in the decision-making process and having equal access to information, products, services, and environments to make informed choices.

Is a mental, not physical, process.

Independence

Being independent does not require having a whole and normal body. Fixating on physical independence leads to limitations on people with disabilities because we underestimate what they are truly capable of.

Promotes better health and emotional wellbeing.

Interdependence

We can only do so much as individuals. Interdependence recognises the value and benefits of doing things with others. Together, we build better communities and environments, which results in happier and healthier individuals.
