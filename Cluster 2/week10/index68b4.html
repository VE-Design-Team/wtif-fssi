Complaints mechanisms

Complaints can be made about disability service providers, their staff, or the services they provide. A person should make a complaint if they have been treated with disrespect, been discriminated against, or had their privacy compromised.

For example, complaints could be made by a person being denied their health information when requested, not being given sufficient information about the services provided, or having their health information shared without their consent.

Anyone can make a complaint, including the service user, their family, friends, their carer, legal representatives or professional advocates. The process should begin with a conversation with the people involved in the incident to see if a resolution can be found. The next step would be to take the matter to management and representatives of the service provider before making a formal complaint to a statutory body.

Individuals Involved

The first step in the complaint process is for the person with a disability, or their advocate, to speak to the staff member involved.

Find out what happened and allow everyone to have their say. The staff member may not be aware that their actions negatively impacted the service user.

This is a chance for the service user to speak up and be heard, and for everyone to come to an understanding and resolution.

Disability Service Representative

If the issue cannot be resolved one-on-one with the people involved, the next step is to speak to management and a representative of the disability service.

All service providers should have formal processes in place for hearing and dealing with complaints.

Statutory Bodies

If the outcome of the disability service provider’s complaint process is not satisfactory, a formal complaint can be lodged with a regulatory body or authority. In the disability sector, most states and territories have statutory bodies that are responsible for managing complaints.

In Victoria, the Disability services Commissioner (DSC) will work with the service user and the provider to find the best resolution. Depending on the severity of the case, the DSC can run an investigation and refer aspects of the complaint to the Victorian Police if necessary.
