How to get service users involved

As a support worker, part of your role is to provide people with all the information they need to make informed decisions about their day to day lives.

Consider how you might do this for a person not able to communicate verbally or who is seemingly confused? You can reflect on their past choices, routines and their history, and this may tell you something about their likely choices.

Sometimes as a support worker, there is a conflict of interest between what your organisation wants and the person’s choices. At these times, you may need to request the services of an independent advocate.

Question 3 of 3

You have a meeting with your supervisor in half an hour. Before that, you need to give one of the residents a shower. The resident is unusually withdrawn today and is taking a lot more time on the toilet. This is preventing you from preparing the shower area and will make you late for the meeting. Which is the most appropriate response?




Incorrect

Deciding for the resident or rushing them could lead to conflict between yourself and the resident. Taking the time to speak and listen to the service user shows them respect and will give the support worker a better understanding of the situation and how to respond. Communicating with your supervisor about the situation is also important as providing quality care is a responsibility they share with you. Allowing service users to participate in making decisions that affect their lives can help to avoid unexpected conflicts with support staff and can help save time and money by ensuring that service users have everything they need early on.
