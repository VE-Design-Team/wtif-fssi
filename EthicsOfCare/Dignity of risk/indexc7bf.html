Dignity of risk

Select each of the headings below to learn more about how dignity of risk and duty of care should be considered when you work in the disability support and aged care industry.

Dignity of Risk

Dignity of risk means respecting each individual’s right to make choices, take risks, make mistakes and learn from them so they can live their life just like any other person in the community.

Dignity of risk does not mean protecting service users from themselves. Depending on the policies and circumstances where you work, you may be asked to assist the service user to smoke, drink an alcoholic beverage, cross-dress so they can go to a party or arrange a sexual encounter. If an activity includes some level of risk, refer to their care plan for information—smoking may need to be supervised, alcohol at certain times of the day may interfere with medication, gender-specific clothing may be stored somewhere other than the wardrobe and certain venues may provide assistive equipment.

Workers in the aged care and disability support industry can infringe upon someone’s dignity of risk by being overly cautious about the duty of care built into their role.

Duty of care

Duty of care refers to the legal responsibility that staff and organisations abide by to do no harm to the people they support. Duty of care requires you to intervene in certain circumstances where:

  • there is a realistic threat of death
  • there is a realistic threat of permanent serious disability
  • a lack of client capacity has been legally declared
  • legally declared involuntary treatment orders or community treatment orders exist, under which someone is receiving treatment even though they may not want it.
