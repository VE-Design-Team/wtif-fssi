Mobility aids

Mobility aids, such as crutches, canes and prosthetics, are forms of AT that have been around for centuries. The materials and mechanisms, from being manually to electrically operated, have changed mobility aids over time.

As we discussed in the last module, there are two main types of mobility aids: physical and sensory or cognitive.

Physical mobility aids

These are devices that support physical movement. Wheelchairs are the most common example. Manual wheelchairs are propelled either by the user physically rolling the wheels or by another person pushing the wheelchair from behind. There are also powered wheelchairs that are electricity-driven and battery-operated.

Other mobility aids include hoists, for lifting people in and out of bed and prosthetics. While hoists can ease the load on workers and reduce injuries (back, neck and other load-related injuries), they have been met with some resistance from both support workers and users. They can be bulky and time-consuming to use, instructions are not always clear and some people feel unsafe being moved by them.

Sensory or cognitive mobility aids

These are devices that help people navigate their environment and include wearable anti-collision devices and bank note recognition devices.

For people with visual impairments, these devices could assist in obstacle avoidance (e.g. stairs, branches, roads, rough surfaces, etc.), orientation and navigation to predetermined locations. At present, most devices are only capable of assisting with either avoidance or navigation, but not both. A cane, for example, is used to avoid obstacles, while a GPS device with audio can guide a person to a destination.

AT for the visually impaired has not surpassed the humble guide dog, which is capable of assisting with both obstacle avoidance and navigation, particularly to known destinations such as the doctor’s office or the shops. Guide dogs can also provide emotional comfort and companionship.
