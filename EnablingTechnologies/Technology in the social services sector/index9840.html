Assistive technology and the social services sector

There are three main stakeholders impacted by the rapidly advancing technology for the social services environment: the support worker (you), the support user (your client) and the support service provider or organisation that you work with. It is important for you to understand how AT is evolving these roles and interrelationships.

We will continue to explore these roles and interrelationships throughout the remainder of this course.

Select each of the headings to learn more about how AT is changing the roles and interrelationships in support services. When you have finished, select again to close the section.
Support worker

AT is changing the role of the support worker by reducing functional support tasks (e.g. dispensing medication, lifting support users in and out of bed or a wheelchair) and creating more time for the delivery of human care factors such as emotional support and communication.

It also requires new forms of guidance in assisting support users to access and train in the use of AT.

Service user

AT can significantly improve the choice, control, independence, well-being and richness in a person’s life.

NDIS funding will favour applications that can clearly outline the benefits of AT compared to the cost of funding it. This means that documenting needs, capabilities and limitations will become increasingly important.

Support service provider

Support service providers need to assist with the provision and training of AT. Staff training may be required for using various forms of AT at an organisational level for communication, record-keeping and management.

Organisations are increasingly making use of ‘mobile offices’ (as opposed to office-based employment arrangements). As a result, there is less structure around working hours. You will need to be able to manage your own day remotely, including scheduling appointments and administrative time.

The way in which you engage with your employer is also evolving. Employers are moving away from paper-based systems towards digital-based systems that require the use of hand-held devices such as smartphones, iPads, apps and cloud-based management systems.

Employers may expect you to use these devices and applications for a variety of functions, including communication, documentation, data and record management and online training. They might even house company policies and procedures online for you to access yourself.
