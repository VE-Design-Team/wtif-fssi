Too many choices and no decisions

Select the images to learn more about the difficulties of having too many options or use the arrows to move through the images.

It is important to bear in mind that even those with the capacity to make wise choices may choose not to choose. The available research shows that many people with psychosocial disabilities would prefer stability to choice. Disruption is to be avoided at all costs. Sometimes there are simply too many choices, leading to confusion and difficulty making a decision. As one parent of a child with cognitive disabilities pointed out to researchers from the University of Melbourne: “she will want something but if you give her too many choices she dithers and is just in a muddle and she cannot make a decision” (Warr, Dickinson and Olney, 2017: 30).

Ironically, research has also shown that in some cases the move toward choice and control for people with disability through the rollout of the NDIS actually led to less choice.

Which ultimately caused existing providers to close down in response to the emergence of new entrants, who had yet to prove their capacity to deliver.

Another participant in the same study pointed out that the new rules under the NDIS enabled service providers to pick and choose “clients who are perceived to be more cooperative and compliant” (ibid 2017: 29).

Finally, it is also important to be aware of the uneven capability of people to negotiate arrangements that they really want. As another respondent to the same study explains: “Philosophically I still think [offering people choice is] the right approach, but a lot of families of people with disabilities didn’t really seem to know actually what they wanted to do because they’re not practiced at making choices, or making real choices. They’re practiced at making choices from what’s available, but not necessarily saying I don’t like anything that you’ve got available for me, this is what I want ‘cause that’s what will make a difference to my quality of life”. 

The flip side of this is that in opening up choice by giving end users money, governments are also opening up opportunities for unscrupulous providers to make a quick buck.

When state and federal governments decided to give vocational education students choice instead of funding vocational providers directly, private companies very quickly emerged and focused on tricking people into choosing education programs. They focused on people with disability. They focused on aboriginal people. They even focused on people living in retirement homes. Sometimes they tricked them by offering a free trip to Bali or a free iPad.

Their trickery was extraordinary in that they even persuaded students to enrol into a diploma at a cost of $20,000 when they could do the very same course up the road at the local TAFE for $4,000. Billions of dollars were lost through one of the biggest social policy scams in our history (Miller and Hayward, 2017).
