Power, individuals and social structures

At its simplest, power involves one person being able to do what they want even against the resistance of others. But power is not just about what one person does to another. It can also be about relationships between groups of people. Class, gender, racially-based groupings are all examples of where struggles can take place between large numbers of people who share certain economic, physical or cultural differences.

We can include those who have a disability versus those who appear not to have one in this category. We can also include those who are old or very young compared to those who are within another age category. These relationships between groups may not be due to the efforts of any single individual or a small number of individuals. They happen because society is structured in a way that places these groups in opposition to one another, not because of nature but because of the exercise of power, which in turn ensures these relationships keep going—sometimes for generations.
