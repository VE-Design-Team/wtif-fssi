Assumptions and ableism

It is often our taken-for-granted assumptions about everyday life that are the source of the most profound discrimination. Racism is an example, as is sexism. Disability theorists have drawn our attention to another way this happens, involving ‘ableist’ assumptions. This is where ‘able-bodied individuals’ judge and exclude people based on bodily or cognitive difference.

Ableism is so common that often people don’t recognise they are engaging in it. As defined by Kumari-Campbell (2008), ableism can be described as the lack of acknowledgement of people with disability and their exclusion in the social sphere, and a preference and bias to ‘the norm and fully formed’ over the bodily or cognitive difference of the disabled (Kumari-Campbell 2008).

Historically

Historically many people with disability have been forcibly sterilised because of the ableist belief that people with disability should not be allowed to be parents.

Pop culture

In popular movies, criminals or bad people often have a disability or bodily difference such as albinism, a limp, or blindness in one eye—reinforcing the belief that disability is something to be feared.

Inspirational

Children with physical disabilities are often described as ‘inspirational’ for going about their daily lives because of the ablest belief that having a disability must be so miserable that just living life is inspirational.

Design

We design our cities in ways that effectively prevent many people with a physical disability from being able to move around. Uneven footpaths can be literally life-threatening. Traffic lights that change too quickly to enable someone in a wheelchair to cross the road are also fundamentally dangerous.
