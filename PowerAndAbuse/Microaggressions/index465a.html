The reality of consequence control

In this scenario you are the service user in a facility for people with disability. Your support worker, who helps you with daily activities, has entered your room this morning to get you ready for the day, but your back is sore, so you tell him you want to take it easy and go slow this morning.

Work through the following scenario to learn more about how consequence control affects victims. This is not an assessment. In each step, select the action you would take. You can try this activity as many times as you like.

Your support worker tells you “Hurry up and get out of bed or you won’t get any breakfast”.

  • You don’t want to miss breakfast, so you hurry up.
  • You don’t care what your support worker says, so you take your time.

You hurt yourself getting out of bed and now you are miserable.

  • Go back and try again.

Your support worker refuses to let you have breakfast. You complain but then he tells you if don’t stop complaining he will remove the TV.

  • You don’t want to lose the TV, so you stop complaining.
  • You keep complaining because you should not be treated like this.

You are hungry and miserable.

  • Go back and try again.

You spend the day hungry and miserable. Your support worker tells you to stop reading your book and go to bed or he’ll turn off the light and leave you in the dark.

  • You don’t want to be left in the dark, so you go to bed.
  • You refuse to go to bed because you want to stay up and read.

You go to bed, but you are not happy.

  • Go back and try again.

The support worker turns off the light and leaves you in a chair in the dark. Congratulations! You now have a much better understanding about how consequence control is not a choice for the victim.
