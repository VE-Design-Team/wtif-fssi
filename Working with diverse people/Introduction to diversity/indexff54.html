Compounding disadvantage

When a person is a member of more than one oppressed group, it is not simply a case of adding up the discrimination together in a straightforward equation. Instead, it is about understanding the complex ways in which the different social divisions of race, gender, class, etc. interrelate and compound. A young person may be living in poverty because he is poorly educated and unable to find work, but his situation is made worse by the fact that he is no longer in communication with his family because they donâ€™t accept his sexual orientation.

Intersectionality also questions the idea that people can be easily divided into two groups in discrimination cases: the oppressors and the oppressed. It is most likely that many people span both, they may experience discrimination because of a characteristic they have while also being part of a privileged group. For example, an elderly, wealthy, white woman with a mental disorder could feel privileged because of her race and class, but be discriminated against for her gender, age and disability.

As a support worker, you need to appreciate that the people you work with may have had different life experiences to your own.
