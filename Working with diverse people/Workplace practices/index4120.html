Four principles for organisational practice

As a support worker you are not working in isolation, but as part of an organisation most of the time. The following principles can be applied to good practice across human service organisations.

Select each of the headings to learn more about the four principles for organisational practice. When you have finished, select again to close the section.

Non-discriminatory

Respect for the inherent dignity and worth of everyone regardless of their class, race/ethnicity, gender, sexuality, age, ability, etc. This underpins a human rights approach. Organisations should ensure that diversity is progressive, valued and reflective of the current diversity in the community.

Accessibility

Services should be welcoming and accessible for all people, physically and psychologically. Services must be responsive to communication and language needs (e.g., assistive technology, Auslan or language interpreters). Toilets and waiting rooms should be fully accessible (e.g., ramps, spatially appropriate). The setting of all facilities should be culturally appropriate to avoid offence to any individual or community.

Participatory

Everyone should be encouraged to participate in the design and production of the services they rely on. Partnership and collaboration involve working alongside people who experience vulnerabilities, discrimination and oppression. The aim is to create interventions and services that reflect everyone’s values and goals. ‘Doing with, not for’ vulnerable people improves participation. Mutual trust is essential to this joint process.

Accountability

An organisation must be equitable and transparent in its provision of services. Service users should be encouraged to provide feedback. Service delivery need to be regularly monitored and evaluated to ensure services are responsive, inclusive and non-discriminatory.
