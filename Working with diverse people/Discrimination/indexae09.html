Personal, cultural, structural discrimination

It is important to recognise that discrimination operates and affects people at the personal, cultural and structural (PCS) levels.

The PCS analysis helps to identify where discrimination is experienced but more importantly, it also suggests what we need to do to address the discrimination.

Select the images on the left to reveal more information about the different types of discrimination in society

Personal

Personal discrimination is when an individual is discriminated against on the basis of particular attributes. Sexist name calling or refusing to serve someone in a shop because of their race are examples of personal discrimination.

Cultural

Cultural discrimination occurs when people make assumptions about or discriminate against entire groups, causing hurt and harm to the whole group. Media profiling or police harassment are examples of cultural discrimination.

Structural

The structures in our society are built by powerful and favoured groups to advantage themselves. When discrimination is part of established practice, policy or law, it makes it very difficult for those groups to lift themselves out of unfortunate circumstances. Examples include the over-imprisonment of Indigenous people or the lack of diversity in high paid and powerful professions like law or politics.
