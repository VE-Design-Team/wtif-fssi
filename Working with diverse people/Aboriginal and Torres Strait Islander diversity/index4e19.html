Social determinants

There is a well-known saying that ‘nothing happens in a vacuum’. This means that no event or situation occurs without a reason. This saying is particularly relevant in relation to the health and well-being of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples. As you have seen in this module, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Australians experience poorer health outcomes throughout their lives compared to non-Indigenous Australians. There must be reasons for this difference. The determinants of health are factors that influence how likely we are to stay healthy or become ill or injured.

The main social determinants of health are:

  • social capital (i.e. the networks of relationships among people who live and work in a particular society)
  • education
  • employment and work
  • housing
  • early life experiences
  • residential environment
  • social exclusion (e.g. as a result of prejudices based on race, religion, gender, or sexual orientation).

The social determinants of health are factors in the outer layers (i.e. general socioeconomic, cultural and environmental conditions; living and working conditions; and social and community networks) that influence individual health determinants in the inner layers. In the diagram below, you can see that the outer social determinants extend inwards to affect other factors.

If we examine any of these social determinants in relation to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples, we can begin to understand why they experience poorer health than the non-Indigenous community. Let’s look at employment for example. The relationship between employment and better health has been well established. In addition, social factors such as those described above may increase a person’s likelihood of engaging in risky health behaviours such as smoking and/or excessive alcohol consumption. These broader social issues must be addressed to achieve lasting gains in Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander health.
