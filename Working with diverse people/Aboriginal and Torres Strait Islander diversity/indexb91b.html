Identity

People who identify themselves as ‘Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander’ range from dark skinned, broad nosed to blonde haired blue-eyed people. Aboriginal people define Aboriginality not by skin colour but by relationships. Light-skinned Aboriginal people often face challenges on their Aboriginal identity because of stereotyping.

For example, many people think that all Aboriginal people have dark skin. Indigenous people who do not fit the physical, social or cultural stereotypes can be excluded by the Indigenous community as opportunists or imposters. Indigenous people with light skin also face discrimination in Australia by the broader non-Indigenous community. Australian governments and Indigenous communities have accepted a three-part system to confirm a person’s Aboriginality or Torres Strait Islander identity.

Of Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander descent

Evidenced by birth records or genealogies verified by a suitable authority. If these records are not available, a letter signed by the chairperson of an Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander incorporated organisation can be used as evidence.

Who identifies as an Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander

E.g. They identify to others that they are Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander.

Is accepted as such by the community in which they live

E.g. Confirmation by the chairperson of an Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander incorporated organisation in the community where the person lives or has previously lived.
