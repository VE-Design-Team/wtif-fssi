Supporting residents

You need to ensure that you approach a resident’s support in a structured way.

Select each of the requirements for providing personal care.

Greet the resident

Knock on the resident’s door and respect their privacy when entering. Introduce yourself and clearly explain the activity you will complete.

It is important that you develop and practice good communication skills to make the resident feel comfortable, safe and informed. Gain your resident’s consent to perform the tasks.

Confirm the resident’s preferences

Talk to the resident throughout the procedure and clarify their personal preferences so adjustments can be made in a timely manner.

The resident may prefer a bath rather than a shower; they may use deodorants and makeup, or dress, shave or wear their hair a certain way. These can be influenced by culture, age or social experience.

Prompt and encourage resident participation

Positively motivate the resident to perform the task and allow them as much time as is needed to complete the task themselves.

Provide positive feedback—this will reinforce their sense of independence, self-worth, empowerment and control.

Offer assistance if the resident struggles to perform any tasks. Follow your organisation’s infection control policy and procedures.

Assess the resident’s strength and mobility

Read the resident’s individual care plan and make allowances for any limitations they may have. A physiotherapist may have assessed your resident before his/her care plan was initiated, but it is your responsibility to monitor and report any mobility changes.

Good personal care involves assessment, quality care delivery and frequent evaluation. Seek assistance from another staff member or use equipment aids (if required) to safeguard the resident’s safety as well as your own.

Safely use and adjust any equipment, aids and appliances.

Maintain the resident’s privacy

Keep the resident covered to preserve their modesty and privacy. Again, consider the resident’s physical and emotional needs. Where possible, keep the resident covered during transfer and the procedure itself. This maintains warmth.

Observe and assess resident health

Note any signs of deterioration including redness, bruising, skin breaks, lesions and abnormalities. Once you have completed the activity, report these changes to the Resident Nurse (RN), document it in the progress notes, and add it to the doctor’s book if instructed to do so.

Attend to special areas

Wash and dry in between skin folds, under breasts, in the perineal area and between fingers and toes.

Note: Diabetes can reduce blood flow and damage nerves to the feet. Special care must be taken to prevent ulcers, infection and amputation. See further resources section below.

Attend to peripherals

Trim nails and wash/dry/style hair as part of grooming, wash dentures and teeth as part of oral hygiene, and ensure glasses and hearing aids are clean and properly in place.

Ensure infection control

To prevent cross infection, dispose of linen in the linen skip, and wash and dry your hands at the end of every procedure.

Aids

Many residents will require the use of specific aids to assist them with their care or mobility. These aids and equipment are designed to make procedures safer and to promote resident independence and dignity.

Aids include such things as lifting hoists, standing machines, shower chairs, princess chairs, ‘pinchers’, specialised shoes and clothing, walking frames, modified utensils, white canes, communication boards, etc. Safe handling policies should be followed at all times.
