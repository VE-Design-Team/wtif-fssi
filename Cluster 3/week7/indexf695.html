Progress notes

While an incident report is incident focused, progress notes are resident focused. For example, if you saw Mr O’Connell fall, you would follow the protocol for a fall. Once Mr O’Connell fell, you would fill out an incident report. You would also record a progress note about Mr O’Connell’s fall and the actions you took. Your progress note would be taken into account when Mr O’Connell’s care plan is updated; for example, he may need more support when walking. Progress notes are required for any observed changes that are not contained in a resident’s care plan. A care plan outlines the resident’s specific needs and needs to be reviewed regularly. The care plan addresses the resident’s needs including:

  • medical
  • nursing
  • mental
  • psycho-social
  • emotional
  • spiritual
  • physical.

Progress notes record any exception (change) to a resident’s normal routine based on their care plan. When writing progress notes, you only need to record a change, not something that follows the resident’s care plan and is part of the normal routine. Progress notes are a legal document and could be used in a court of law. For this reason, it is important that you fill out progress notes correctly. This means:

  • Checking that you are writing in the relevant person’s notes (look for identifiers like name and date of birth).
  • Writing clearly with a blue or black pen.
  • Signing and dating your entry.
  • Recording the time of your entry in 24hr clock (e.g. 1420hrs NOT 2.20pm).
  • Entering notes in chronological order.
  • Entering progress notes at the time any change is noticed.
  • Do not leave blank lines or pages. Draw a line through any blank spaces to ensure that no-one can insert notes.
  • If you continue from one page to another, write ‘Note continues overleaf’ at the bottom of the page and then ‘Continues from previous page’ at the top of the next page. This will ensure that the reader knows that the note continues across multiple pages and that they won’t miss any important information.
  • If you make a mistake, rule a line through the incorrect information. Write a reason for ruling the information out (e.g. ‘These notes written in wrong resident file. Details re-entered in correct resident’s notes’). Make sure you sign and date this correction.
  • Stick to the facts, do not make personal judgements. (e.g. ‘Dot smelled strongly of urine’ NOT ‘Dot was incontinent’. Dot may smell of urine because she sat in a chair where someone else had urinated)
