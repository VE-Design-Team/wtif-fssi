De-escalating behaviours of concern

De-escalating behaviours of concern allow you to avoid crises and remain in control. Organised thinking, person-centred approach and good communication form de-escalation techniques that can help you avoid a potential crisis.

Select the headings below to learn more about de-escalating behaviours of concern.

Assess the situation

  • Is the resident an immediate danger to self or others?
  • What type of behaviour is the resident displaying? (Is the resident upset, angry, frightened, etc.)
  • Who is being affected and how?

Refer to the care plan

  • What do we know about the resident?
  • What can you discover that might be triggering this behaviour and what can immediately be done to calm the resident?

Talk to the resident

It’s important that you listen actively and respond with empathy. Consider the following approach:

  • Give the resident your undivided attention so they feel validated and important.
  • Be non-judgemental and mindful of your body language and tone of voice.
  • Focus on the resident’s feelings, not just the facts.
  • Use a calm, quiet voice and positive body language.
  • Acknowledge the resident’s frustration; imagine it from their perspective.
  • Remove possible causes of stress.
  • Take the resident to a quiet place or remove onlookers and reduce stimuli etc.
  • Meet the resident’s underlying need.
  • Distract the resident with an activity they enjoy.

Stay flexible in your approach

Techniques that work once may not work next time because people living with dementia have good days and bad days, just like the rest of us.

When something doesn’t work, ask yourself “What else can I try?”

Alert

If you are unable to calm the resident, ask your supervisor for help.
