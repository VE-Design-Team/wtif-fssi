Categories of dementia symptoms

Symptoms of dementia are often grouped into categories called stages, which consists of: early, moderate and advanced.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about the symptoms in each category.
  • Early
  • Moderate
  • Advanced

Symptoms are subtle and may not be immediately obvious. The service user may begin to sense that something is not right. Family and friends may begin to question and comment on the changes they notice. The service user may become:

  • more forgetful of details and recent events
  • repetitive or lose thought during conversation
  • indifferent to social conventions (ignore and interrupt)
  • embarrassed or frightened when they recognise changes in their memory or thinking
  • disinterested in hobbies and activities
  • self-centred and less concerned with others’ feelings
  • unwilling to try new things.

Problems are more obvious and disabling. They may become:

  • more relaxed, inclined to give in, and withdraw
  • confused regarding time and place
  • very repetitive
  • see or hear things that are not there
  • lost if away from familiar surroundings
  • confused with names of family or friends
  • behave inappropriately
  • muddled and hard to understand
  • neglectful of hygiene or eating
  • preoccupied with the past, thinking back to happy times, restoring old memories and sometimes living in that time and reality
  • frustrated, angry and distressed
  • preoccupied with their memories, ‘living’ almost entirely in that time and reality.

This is the third and final stage. The service user is severely disabled and needs total care. Their sense of touch, and their ability to hear and respond to emotion remains. They may:

  • lose their ability to understand or use speech
  • not respond when someone walks into the room or speaks to them
  • not recognise friends and family
  • be incontinent
  • become restless, have uncontrollable movements or permanently immobile
  • be disturbed at night
  • feel threatened and react aggressively
  • be confined to their bed in the final weeks or months of life.
