Identity controversy

It is important to be aware that confirming Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander identity remains a controversial and challenging issue for many Indigenous people and communities. There are issues with the three-part system for defining Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander identity. For example, how does one confirm one’s Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander identity when no records are available?

What happens when a person only meets one or two of the criteria? Indigenous Australians who cannot provide documented proof of Aboriginality or Torres Strait Islander identify may be refused access to a range of services, including the fundamental needs of health, housing and legal aid to which they may be legitimately entitled to.

Some Aboriginal or Torres Strait Islander peoples will not identify as such because they fear they will experience racism and discrimination if they do so. A fair-skinned Aboriginal person, for example, may choose not to identify because they think it is safer for them. Many Aboriginal persons carry certificates from Indigenous organisations which state their Aboriginality. Can you imagine any other group in Australia who needs to carry proof of their race? For many Aboriginal people, this is discriminatory and insulting.
