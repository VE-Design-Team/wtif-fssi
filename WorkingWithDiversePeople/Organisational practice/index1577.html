Cultural safety

A culturally safe environment is one in which diverse groups people can work together without fear of being discriminated against or oppressed because of their ethnic or individual differences. Instead, people’s cultural identities are recognised, respected and celebrated.

Services may need to adapt their approach based on feedback from service users. Service users should not feel intimidated or fear mistreatment from support services for voicing their concerns. Engagement in decision-making and in co-designing services improves the feeling of safety and respect.

Organisations can foster cultural safety in their workplaces by encouraging employees to consider, not only feedback from service users, but also peer reviews with colleagues and supervisors. Other ways include having cultural mentors and mutual learning with service users and colleagues, where individuals share their histories and cultural backgrounds to gain a deeper appreciation and understanding of others.

Cultural safety is a process. It begins with cultural awareness, cultural sensitivity and cultural competence. Practice identifying what each of these terms means in the following activity.

Cultural Awareness

Acknowledging and understanding ethnic and individual differences.

Cultural Sensitivity

Involves self-exploration, by examining how our own values and beliefs have been shaped by our families, communities and experiences and how these influence our behaviours and actions towards others.

Cultural Competence

Being respectful of other people’s cultural values, beliefs and traditions and being responsive to their different cultural practices and linguistic needs.

Cultural Safety

Working with diverse people by collaborating on designs and decisions, learning about the history of the people you work with and the impact the organisation’s services have had on them and treating people with respect regardless of their cultural or individual differences.
