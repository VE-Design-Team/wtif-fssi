Principles for organisational practice

As a support worker you are not working in isolation, but as part of an organisation. Just as there are principles for good practice at the individual level, there are also principles at the organizational level for creating support services that promote diversity and inclusion. These principles are non-discrimination, accessibility, participation and accountability.

Non-discriminatory

Services should pursue policies, procedures and practices that respect the inherent dignity and worth of everyone regardless of their class, race/ethnicity, gender, sexuality, age, ability, etc.

This means having no tolerance for discrimination and creating spaces that make diverse groups of people feel welcome and valued.

Accessibility

Services should be welcoming and be accessible to all people. This means that an organisation’s services are available in multiple forms to accommodate a diverse population. For example, providing assistive technology and Auslan or language interpreters to meet various communication and language needs.

Another example is having fully accessible physical spaces, like toilets and waiting rooms, that incorporate ramps, elevators and are spatially appropriate for people with different mobilities. The setting of all facilities, such as the artwork on the walls, screen saver images on computers or having private spaces for religious prayer, should be culturally appropriate to avoid offence to any individual or community.

Participatory

The service user is at the centre of their support. Everyone should be encouraged to participate in the co-design and co-production of the services they rely on. This means partnering and collaborating with people who experience vulnerabilities, discrimination and oppression to create interventions and services that reflect their values and goals.

‘Doing with, not for’ vulnerable people involves mutual trust between the service users and support workers. This is achieved when support workers create safe and trusted environments, understand the needs of the service user, and can adapt to changing requirements over time. It is not just about providing quality support but cooperating with others to be flexible in responding to the service user.

Accountability

An organisation must be equitable and transparent in the services it provides. Service users should be encouraged to provide feedback, with service delivery regularly monitored and evaluated to ensure they are responsive, inclusive and non-discriminatory.
