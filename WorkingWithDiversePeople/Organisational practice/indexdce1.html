A more diverse workplace

Working with difference and diversity requires examining ourselves as support workers as well as the organisation where we work. It also means recognising that discrimination and oppression are structural issues.

No amount of changes at just the personal level is going to reduce classism, sexism, racism, ableism, ageism and heterosexism unless structural change occurs. We have a legal and moral duty to ensure that support human service practice is non-discriminatory. Consider the following questions about how organisations can foster diversity in the workplace.

Question 1 of 3

A service user does not identify as male or female and prefers to be addressed by gender neutral pronouns. You are unaware and refer to the service user as ‘she’. The service user is offended and upset. Once you know why the service user is upset, how should you respond?




Incorrect

This avoids the issue and could create tension between yourself and the service user because you are not acknowledging one of their identities.

Correct

People should be encouraged to ask questions and be open to learning and educating each other about their differences in the workplace. It is important that both parties are willing to have a conversation, especially when others may not be aware or understand how they might have offended another. This is a chance to learn more about each other’s identities and how to be respectful.

Incorrect

This is disrespectful to the service user. As a support worker, your role is to help the service user meet their needs and goals and that involves getting to know them and acknowledging and respecting each other’s differences. This is important for creating a diverse and inclusive environment.

Question 2 of 3

How can organisations attract and keep diverse staff and service users in the long-term?




Incorrect

While it is important to have a clean and safe work environment, having a visually beautiful waiting area is not as critical as listening to people and responding to their needs.

Correct

Listening to people and responding to feedback is one of the best ways to attract and keep diverse staff.

Incorrect

Policies and procedures can be updated but they could have negative effect if they are made without consideration to a diverse group of service users.

Question 3 of 3

A cultural holiday calendar can promote diversity in the workplace.



Correct

Having people share their religious and cultural holidays on a workplace calendar is good way of breaking down stereotypes and assumptions. It provides an informal opportunity for people to talk about their beliefs, share food and traditions and for others in the workplace to help them celebrate.

Incorrect

Unless it was ignored by everyone, a culturally diverse holiday calendar would help to increase cultural awareness, by prompting people to ask questions about what the different holidays mean.
