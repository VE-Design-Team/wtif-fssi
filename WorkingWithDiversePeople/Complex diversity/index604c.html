Multiple identities and Intersectionality

Multiple identities

Often people do not wish to be defined by a single attribute or aspect of their life; they see themselves as having multiple identities. For example, a woman may see herself as a daughter, a doctor, a refugee, a wife, in the privileged upper class, middle-aged and a cancer survivor. Her view of herself may also change over time as she becomes a mother and a volunteer. When you meet a person, keep in mind that they may consider themselves to have multiple identities and do not wish to be identified as just one thing. For example, a person in a wheelchair is so much more than just a person in a wheelchair.

Intersectionality

Intersectionality is when different aspects of a person’s identity expose them to overlapping forms of marginalisation and discrimination. For example, someone could come from a background of poverty, be poorly educated and belong to the LGBTIQ community. They may need help because of their financial situation, which is made more difficult because they receive no support from their family, who no longer communicate with them because of their sexuality.
