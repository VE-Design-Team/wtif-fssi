Five principles for individual practice

Select each of the headings to learn more about the five principles for individual practice. When you have finished, select again to close the section.

Anti-discriminatory practice

Anti-discriminatory practice works towards eliminating sexist, racist, ageist, disablist and heterosexist behaviours and actions. It aims to remove discrimination and oppression by challenging poor practices in the workplace.

Legal context

Understand the law, identify areas that are protected by the law and be aware of how to apply the law in the appropriate jurisdictions (federal, state or local).

Understanding self

Understand your own values and know where they fit within your role as a service provider. It is important to understand where you are in relation to power and privilege and where you are situated politically and socially.

For example, you may be from a wealthy community or a large religious group in your native country. You may have been in powerful position. This may affect your values and judgements about others.

Reflective and reflexive practice

The core elements of anti-discrimination and inclusiveness involve reflexive (present) and reflective (past) practice. Reflexive practice invites you to be engaged in the moment. Consider what you are doing, thinking or feeling. Identify how you are positioned or located in events that affect your service users. Reflective practice involves reviewing an event to reconsider what happened. What was your thinking? What were your feelings? What worked well? Could things have been done differently or better for your service users?

Professional education and training

Working with diversity and difference requires a broad knowledge of issues faced by a range of service users. It requires continual learning of professional skills and ongoing development of values that are helpful for working more effectively.
