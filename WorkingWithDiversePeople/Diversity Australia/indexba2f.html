Marginalised groups

Marginalised groups are groups of people that do not have the same access to rights, resources and opportunities as the rest of the population. For example, many people with mental health issues do not have the same ability to access education, employment and other aspects of society as those who do not have mental health issues. In addition, many people from marginalised groups experience discrimination from the wider community. This is a form of social exclusion and results in these people being ‘left out’. While changes in legislation have helped promote equality, many people still fear discrimination.

People from marginalised groups often have ‘protective factors’. These are attributes or conditions that give them resilience and ‘protect’ them from negative impacts, like stress and risks caused by discrimination. One example of a protective factor is having a safe and comfortable environment. This could be a religious space, like a church or a mosque, or simply spending time with family. Your role in any work you do is to provide clients from marginalised groups with the same opportunities and support that you would give to all Australians. Try to find as much as you can about the particular needs and backgrounds of the people you work with. It is important that you are also aware of the ways that experiences of discrimination can affect clients and shape the way they may respond to certain situations. For example, someone who has experienced discrimination in a medical setting because they are gay may not want to seek medical attention, even when it is necessary.
