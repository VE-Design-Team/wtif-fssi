Self-reflection

Your identity is formed by the values and beliefs you hold about life, yourself and others. These values and beliefs also affect how you behave. We are all born within the culture of a family, society, religion and place. We learn this culture during the very early stages of our lives. Each society influences the beliefs and values of its members. From our parents, communities and the media, we learn social and cultural perspectives and biases. Social and cultural biases arise when we consider our behaviour ‘normal’ and use that to judge others. Our beliefs and values can change over time, depending on the choices we make and on our experiences. In any role you have, you will be working with colleagues from a range of culturally diverse backgrounds. You may also work with diverse members of the public. It is important that you develop self-awareness so that you work with everyone in an inclusive and respectful way. Developing self-awareness will help you to learn more about yourself. To help you to truly value and respect diversity, take some time to reflect on your own culture, values and beliefs. Reflecting will help you to appreciate your culturally diverse colleagues and residents and to work more effectively with them.

Select each of the areas of your culture to learn some ways to reflect.

Personal identity

Your personal identity is the concept you develop of yourself. It evolves and changes over the course of your life. You can think about your personal identity by asking yourself the following questions:

  • Who am I?
  • What am I?
  • How do I see the world?
  • How would I describe myself?
  • What makes me happy?
  • What makes me sad?
  • What annoys me?
  • Why do I react in these ways?

Language

Think about the language you spoke and listened to while you were growing up. Think of that language in terms of dialect, accent, use of slang, general tone of the language, influences on the language and history of the language. Reflecting on your language helps you to understand the connection other people have to their own language.

Cultural attitudes to pain, illness and death

How are the concepts of pain, illness, and death experienced in your culture?

  • How are these concepts discussed in your community?
  • What information is okay to share about pain, illness and death and what is not okay to share?
  • What emotions are associated with these concepts in your culture?
  • When and how would someone in pain ask for help?

Community structure

Think about the family structures in your community as well as any times when the community comes together. Can you think of particular community groups? Are there any hierarchical structures in the community (i.e. do some members of the community hold more power and influence than others)? For example, does your community have patriarchal or matriarchal influences, is respecting elders very important, etc.?

Community rules

Think about the rules that your community expects its members to follow. These may include social etiquette, manners, things that are considered polite or impolite, asking permission from elders, observing cultural holidays, cultural traditions and superstitions.

Social awareness

Social awareness is thinking about how others see you and how your actions make them feel. How do you think others view you? How do you view others? How are you limited by your cultural biases?

Improving social awareness

Think about yourself and your views of others in terms of your strengths and limitations. In this way, you can educate yourself about unfamiliar customs and cultural behaviours. You can change your behaviour towards others and respond to them with understanding. Improving your social and self-awareness will help you work more effectively with colleagues and the public.
