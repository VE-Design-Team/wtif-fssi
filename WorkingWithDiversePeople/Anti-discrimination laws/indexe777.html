Laws and policies

It is important for support workers to understand that sometimes laws and policies are themselves discriminatory.

Example

The enduring power of attorney for an older person with dementia may be able to legally force the person to move out of his/her own home, despite the person being safe and comfortable, with no unacceptable risk or unsustainable cost to themselves or others. An enduring power of attorney (financial) is effectively the deciding voice of the represented person in legal matters. Similarly, an enduring power of attorney (medical) can authorise treatments, even against the wishes of the represented patient, especially if his/her wishes are not documented.

Example

Support workers must participate in ongoing professional development to keep up-to-date with relevant laws and regulations. Some service workers even become involved in continuing law reform, policy development and/or research and evaluation.

Example: In recent years parliaments in all Australian states and territories have enacted mandatory reporting laws to address child protection, disability abuse, aged care and domestic violence. These laws are not the same across all jurisdictions. The main differences concern ‘who’ is obliged to report and what types of abuse and neglect must be reported.
