Understanding self

It’s easy to think that our own values and ways of living are the normal and natural ways of thinking and being. However, your role as a support worker is to support people to live the lives that they want to live, according to their values, not your own.

Understanding yourself, why you live the way you do, why you make the choices you make, and why you have followed the career that you have, helps you to better understand others. A big part of understanding self is reflecting on your own social position – in what ways are you privileged and in what ways are you oppressed?

It is only through a deep understanding of our own values and social position that we can ensure we are not engaging in discriminatory practice.

There are several principles that can help us explore our own identities in relation to support work.

Social Difference

As a support work, it is important to look at inequalities of power you may have with the service user. These differences may include race, class, gender, sexual preference, disability and age.

For example, consider the potential imbalance in care of two residents in an aged care facility. One resident is an educated third generation Australian woman and the second resident is a Sudanese immigrant man who speaks very little English.

Linking the Personal and Political

This means valuing the person despite commonly held stereotypical and negative views around their difference.

For example, there is the negative stereotype of the Sudanese community being involved in violent gang crimes. When working with the Sudanese resident, it is important to see him as the person he is, rather than projecting the negative stereotype onto him.

Power

This concerns a person’s access to resources. Support workers have a duty to ensure access is equal for everyone.

For example, the Australian resident may be able to exercise greater control over her living conditions because she can communicate effectively with staff and has a good understanding of the Australian support systems.

The Sudanese resident’s lower English skills make it difficult for him to voice his decisions and concerns and to understand the culture and workings of the facility. Providing an interpreter would ensure that he is able to share his thoughts and access the same information and services as the Australian resident.

Historical and Geographical Location

A person’s background will help define who that person is. This information can guide how you should best engage with the person in a manner that is sensitive to their values, wants, and needs.

For example, knowing the backgrounds of both residents would improve your interactions with them. There may be certain cultural customs and traditions that they follow or topics that they are uncomfortable with (e.g. medical issues) that require extra care when having discussions with them about their support.

Mutual Involvement

This is the continual consideration of how your own values, social difference and power may affect your interaction with others.

For example, reviewing your own behaviours and actions towards others. Are you treating both residents equally with the same patience and consideration? Are you ensuring they have the same access to information, services and opportunities regardless of their backgrounds and personal characteristics?
