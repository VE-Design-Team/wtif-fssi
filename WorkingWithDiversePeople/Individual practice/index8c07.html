Reflective and reflexive practice

Reflective practice involves reviewing an event and considering what happened, our thoughts and feelings at the time, what worked well and what could have been done differently to improve the care of the service user. The goal is to learn from past experiences by comparing what we wanted to achieve with what the actual outcome was in the situation.

For example, you accompany Fred, a young man with Down Syndrome to the football, an activity that he normally enjoys. On this particular day, he appears agitated and not engaged with the football and asks to leave early. Reflective practice involves looking back and asking; why did this event go badly when it is normally successful? Did anything unusual happen at the football? Are there other things going on in Fred’s life that might have impacted his mood?

Reflexive practice invites us to be engaged in the present. What are you doing, thinking, feeling and how are you positioned in this encounter? While reflective practice means stepping away from an event and thinking about it afterwards, reflexive practice is evaluating what is happening now and changing things as you go.

For example, when you were at the football, if you were engaged in reflexive practice you would be asking yourself: How am I supporting Fred? Am I focused on his needs or distracted? What is my mood like? Could I be contributing to his bad mood? Am I deeply listening to him?

Language

Think about the language you spoke and listened to while you were growing up. Think of that language in terms of dialect, accent, use of slang, general tone of the language, influences on the language and history of the language. Reflecting on your language helps you to understand the connection other people have to their own language.

Cultural attitudes to pain, illness, and death

  • How are the concepts of pain, illness, and death experienced in your culture?
  • How are these concepts discussed in your community?
  • What information is okay to share about pain, illness and death and what is not okay to share?
  • What emotions are associated with these concepts in your culture?
  • When and how would someone in pain ask for help?

Community structure

Think about the family structures in your community as well as any times when the community comes together. Can you think of particular community groups?

Are there any hierarchical structures in the community? (i.e. do some members of the community hold more power and influence than others).

For example, does your community have patriarchal or matriarchal influences, is respecting elders very important, etc?

Community rules

Think about the rules that your community expects its members to follow. These may include social etiquette, manners, things that are considered polite or impolite, asking permission from elders, observing cultural holidays, cultural traditions and superstitions.

Social awareness

Social awareness is thinking about how others see you and how your actions make them feel. How do you think others view you? How do you view others? How are you limited by your cultural biases?

Improving social awareness

Think about yourself and your views of others in terms of your strengths and limitations. In this way, you can educate yourself about unfamiliar customs and cultural behaviours.

You can change your behaviour towards others and respond to them with understanding. Improving your social and self-awareness will help you work more effectively with colleagues and the public.

Recognising discrimination

When interacting with others in the workplace, ask yourself the following questions to check if you are seeing or treating others unfairly:

  • Do I see the disability rather than the person?
  • Do I see this person as an inconvenience?
  • What ways does society make this person’s life harder?
  • Do I see potential for this person to be happy?
  • Do I consider this person to be lazy?
