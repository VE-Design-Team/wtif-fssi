Common issues with the female reproductive system

Select each of the headings below to learn more about some of the common issues with this system.

Vaginal Thrush

Vaginal thrush is a common fungal infection in the vagina, caused by normally harmless yeast cells (called ‘candida’) that multiply beyond normal levels. Symptoms of vaginal thrush include vaginal burning, itching, stinging and a white discharge from the vagina. Thrush is not deemed a sexually transmitted infection as candida is common in many people. However, it can be passed on during sex. Thrush can also occur in the mouth and anus.

Cancers of the Female Reproductive System

There are many cancers that affect different parts of the female reproductive system, including cancers of the uterus, ovaries, cervix, breasts, fallopian tubes, vagina and vulva. Collectively, cancers of the female reproductive system are called gynaecological cancers. Like other cancers, they occur when abnormal cells develop and reproduce.

Endometriosis

Endometriosis is a painful, chronic condition in which cells from the lining of the uterus flow backward into the fallopian tubes or pelvic organs (rather than flowing forward through menstruation) and begin to grow. As they cannot be shed normally via menstruation, over time these cells bleed, become inflamed, and heal to create scar tissue, causing painful periods and reduced fertility. Endometriosis occurs in up to 10% of the female population.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Pelvic inflammatory disease (PID) is a bacterial infection and inflammation of one or more female reproductive organs. The infection is often sexually transmitted, but can also occur following abortion, childbirth, with the use of internal contraceptive devices, or after a ruptured appendix or bowel infection. Symptoms include abdominal pain, menstrual problems, fever, and pain during intercourse. PID can cause scarring of the fallopian tubes leading to blockage and infertility.

Infertility

Infertility in women is due to problems in one of three areas: ovulation, conception, or carrying a foetus to term. It can be due to age, genetic or biological issues, environmental effects, or lifestyle and diet. While some fertility problems have no cure and will prevent a woman from every conceiving or carrying a child, many can be addressed using surgery or fertility treatments to increase the chances of falling pregnant or carrying a baby to term.
