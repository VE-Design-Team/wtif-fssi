The special senses

Humans have the ability to sense, understand and interact with the world around them. We do this using two separate processes, called sensation and perception. Sensation is the process by which our sense organs (the eyes, ears, nose, tongue and nerves) gather raw information about our environment and surroundings (stimulus) and send this information to the brain. This is made possible through the five basic senses of sight, hearing, smell, taste and touch.

Perception is the process by which the brain makes meaning of the raw information from the senses. The brain selects, organises and interprets the information transmitted by the sense organs. For instance, sound waves that make contact with parts of the ear might be perceived by the brain as either music or a car horn. While light waves that hit the retina might be interpreted as a particular colour, shape or pattern.

The five basic human senses

  • vision
  • hearing (audition)
  • smell (olfaction)
  • taste
  • touch.

Vision

The sense organs used for sight are the eyes. Light hits the retina, travels through the eye and is turned into electrical impulses. These signals are carried to the brain via the optic nerve. The brain then translates these impulses into visual images.

Hearing (Audition)

The sense organs used for hearing are the ears. Air waves are funnelled into the ear causing vibrations that knock against a series of small bones, fluid-filled canals and tiny hairs in the inner ear. These vibrations reach the auditory nerve, which sends them to the brain where they are interpreted as sounds.

Smell (Olfaction)

The sense organ used for olfaction is the nose. Particles of gas in the air are drawn into the nose when we sniff or inhale. These particles travel past receptors in the nose to the olfactory nerve, which then converts information about the particles to electrical impulses that the brain interprets as different smells.

Taste (Gustation)

The main sense organ used for gustation is the tongue, which is helped by sensory receptors in the nose, mouth and throat. Food molecules enter the mouth and are distributed across small bumps on the top of the tongue called taste buds.

Taste buds are distributed throughout the tongue and throat, and can differentiate between four main tastes of sweet, sour, salty and bitter.

Touch

The main sense organ for touch is the skin. Millions of nerve endings in the skin respond to three different types of touch: pressure, temperature and pain. Nerve endings in the skin carry information through the nerves, up the spinal cord and to the brain.

As mentioned earlier, nerves are found in almost every muscle, tissue and organ in the body, allowing pain and pressure to be experienced internally as well as externally.
