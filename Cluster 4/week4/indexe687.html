Tissues

In humans, individual cells with a similar structure and function group together to form body tissue. Body tissue is a collection of cells that all serve a similar purpose. There are four types of human body tissue. Each has a special function that contributes to the overall health and maintenance of the body.

Muscle tissue

Muscle tissue keeps us upright, allows us to move our body parts, and pumps blood, fluids, and food around our body. Some muscle tissue is operated consciously (voluntary) – like when you choose to flex a bicep or turn your head – and others operate automatically (involuntary) – like the fibres that make your heart beat or your intestines contract to move food through your body’s digestive tract.

Connective tissue

Connective tissue works to hold some body parts together and to separate others. It can keep certain parts rigid and in place and allow others to move and reposition. It is also used to insulate, protect, and transport substances within the body. Connective tissue is found everywhere in the body, and comes in many forms including blood, bones, body fat, cartilage, ligaments and tendons.

Epithelial tissue

Epithelial tissue is a form of protective barrier that covers the body’s surface (in the form of skin), lines certain internal cavities, and forms particular glands. Epithelial cells are tightly grouped and act as barriers to the transmission of fluids, microbes and other entities. Epithelial tissue such as skin stops bodily fluids leaking out and potentially harmful bacteria getting in.

Nervous tissue

Nervous tissue makes up the central nervous system (CNS) and includes the brain, spinal cord, and nerves. The main function of nervous tissue is to regulate bodily functions by integrating, controlling and transmitting information between the various parts of the body. Nervous tissue regulates and transmits electrical impulses between the brain and spinal cord, making it possible for us to think, see, hear, feel, breathe, and move.
