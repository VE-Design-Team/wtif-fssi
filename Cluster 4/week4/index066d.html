Parts of the respiratory system

Select the + to learn more about the different parts that make up the respiratory system.
  • Nose and mouth: The nasal and oral (mouth) cavities form the two openings of the respiratory system. The nasal cavity is designed to warm and moisturise air before it reaches the lungs. It is lined with hairs and mucus that filter contaminants before they are taken inside the body. The mouth isn’t as effective as the nose at warming and moisturising air; however, it allows air to be taken in more quickly and at larger volumes when needed, such as during exercise.
  • Pharynx: The pharynx (or throat) plays a part in both the respiratory and digestive systems. It is a funnel-like tube that connects both the nose and the back of the mouth to organs used for swallowing and breathing. The pharynx has a flap of tissue called the epiglottis that acts like a switch to direct food and liquids to the oesophagus and air to the larynx.
  • Larynx: The larynx, also known as the ‘voice box’, connects the pharynx to the trachea. The larynx controls the cough reflex, which is designed to prevent foreign objects (like food or liquid) from entering and damaging the lungs. The other function of the larynx is sound. It contains a pair of vocal folds that vibrate as air passes over them, creating the sounds needed for speech, singing, and other noises. Muscles in the larynx expand and contract to change the pitch and volume of these sounds.
  • Trachea: The trachea, or windpipe, provides a direct pathway for air to enter and exit the lungs. It is lined with smooth, moist mucus tissues and tiny hairlike projections called cilia. These trap particles of dust and other contaminants before they reach the lungs. Contaminated mucus is moved back through the trachea to the pharynx, where it can be either swallowed or coughed up and spat or sneezed out.
  • Bronchi: The base of the trachea splits into two branches called ‘bronchi’: one bronchiole carries air in and out of the left lung, and one carries air in and out of the right lung. The bronchi regulate airflow by dilating and contracting as required. During periods of exercise the muscles relax to allow more air to pass in and out of the lungs. During periods of inactivity or rest they contract to prevent hyperventilation.
  • Lungs: The lungs are a pair of spongy, air-filled organs on either side of the chest. Because of its proximity to the heart, the left lung is slightly smaller than the right, and has only two lobes; whereas the right lung is larger and three lobed. Millions of capillaries called alveoli surrounded by capillaries exchange gases that allow oxygen to be taken into the bloodstream while depositing carbon dioxide as waste that is exhaled back into the air.
