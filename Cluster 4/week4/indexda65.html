Body systems

In the previous section we looked at the structures that make up the human organism, ranging from simple cells through to basic tissue types and then more complex organs. When two or more individual organs work together to perform a specific function, it becomes an even more complex organisation called a ‘body system’. These organisations are also referred to as ‘organ systems’ or ‘biological systems’, but for the purposes of this unit we will refer to them as body systems. Every human body comprises a variety of body systems working independently, or together, to keep us alive and healthy.

Many body systems overlap in their functions, performing multiple roles across multiple arrangements. For instance, the respiratory system, which includes the mouth, nose, trachea and lungs, allows us to take in necessary oxygen and expel carbon dioxide; however, it requires the circulatory system, comprising heart and blood vessels, to transport the oxygen and carbon dioxide around our body and deliver it to where it needs to go. It is vital that each component of a system does its individual job properly in order to keep the wider system operating effectively.

Muscular System

Controls movement, and helps to move substances such as blood and food around the body.

Nervous System

Allows all cells, tissues, muscles, organs and body systems to communicate with one another. It consists of the brain, spinal cord, nerves, and nerve endings.

Lymphatic System

A network of delicate tubes, nodes and organs that works closely with both the circulatory system and the immune system.

Skeletal System

Comprises all the bones in the human body, along with a network of cartilage, tendons and ligaments that connect the bones.

Circulatory System

Moves blood around the body, transporting oxygen, nutrients, hormones, and waste products to various organs.

Digestive System

Converts food into basic nutrients that the body can use for energy, and expels waste.

Urinary System

Also known as the renal system, it makes, stores, and expels waste products and excess water produced by the body.

Male Reproductive System

Allows humans to procreate, give birth, and pass on genetic material to future generations.

Female Reproductive System

Allows humans to procreate, give birth, and pass on genetic material to future generations.

Endocrine System

Includes all of the glands in the body, as well as the hormones produced by those glands.

Respiratory System

Takes in oxygen (O2) from the air, and expels carbon dioxide (CO2) from the body as waste. It also allows us to speak and communicate.

Integumentary System

Its name comes from the word ‘integument’, which refers to an organism’s natural covering, like a skin, husk, shell, or rind. The integumentary system acts to protect the body from various kinds of damage and includes hair and nails.
