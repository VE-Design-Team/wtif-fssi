Four basic parts of a cell

The most basic units that make up all living organisms are cells. Some organisms, like bacteria or amoebas, are unicellular, meaning they consist of only one cell. Others, like plants and humans, are multi-cellular and made up of many cells. Cells themselves can be broken down into smaller parts. Cells are considered the building blocks of life as they are the smallest unit capable of reproducing independently.

There are over 37 trillion cells in the human body. Cells need energy to function and reproduce. Depending on the type of cell, energy is sourced from sunlight or food molecules such as fats, carbohydrates, and proteins. These source molecules pass through the cell membrane and are converted into energy through processes such as ‘photosynthesis’ in plants, and ‘cellular respiration’ in animals. Excess energy is stored for later use. While there are many types of cells that form different parts of an organism, all cells – no matter what their purpose – have four basic components.

Cell membrane

Surrounds the cell and contains its contents.

Cytoplasm

A watery substance inside the membrane.

Ribosomes

Floating in the cytoplasm, ribosomes make protein.

DNA

Contains the genetic instructions for what the cell will become and how it will behave.
