Common issues with the cardiovascular system

Select each of the headings below to learn more about some of the common issues with the cardiovascular system.

Coronary Heart Disease (CHD)

Coronary heart disease, or CHD, is the leading cause of death in Australia, claiming the life of one Australian every 27 minutes. CHD involves a narrowing of the arteries through a build-up of fatty substances called ‘plaque’. Plaque build-up reduces the flow of blood to the heart, and can eventually cause a blockage that leads to angina, permanent damage to the heart muscle, and even heart attack.

Causes of CHD include lifestyle factors such as inactivity, being overweight, smoking, and unhealthy diet; as well as co-morbid conditions such as hypertension and diabetes.

  1. Angina is pain or discomfort caused by insufficient flow of blood to the heart. It can vary from mild to severe, and be experienced during periods of activity or rest. Angina is not a heart attack, but those who experience angina are at increased risk of heart attack.
  2. A heart attack occurs when there is a complete blockage in an artery that stops blood reaching the heart. Reduced oxygen to heart muscles causes the tissue to start to die. It can be experienced as an extreme pain, tightness or pressure in the chest, or have less obvious symptoms like mild discomfort, shortness of breath or nausea.

Hypertension

Hypertension is more commonly known as high blood pressure. It can occur as a result of stress, poor lifestyle or diet, or due to an underlying condition such as kidney disease.

It is more common in those who are older, due to gradual build-up of plaque in the arteries. Hypertension rarely produces any symptoms, but if left undetected can cause stroke, aneurysm, and damage to the heart, kidneys and other organs.

Whereas normal blood pressure involves a reading of 120/80, hypertension is indicated when this reading exceeds 130/80. A person with high blood pressure is said to be hypertensive.

Blood Disorders

Blood disorders don’t affect the organs of the cardiovascular system, they affect blood itself. Different disorders cause different problems related to red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets and plasma. The most common blood disorders include:

  • anaemia (a lack of red blood cells, causing fatigue, paleness and shortness of breath)
  • blood cancers such as leukemia and lymphoma (caused by abnormal white blood cells)
  • haemophilia (a genetic disorder that stops blood from clotting properly).

Varicose Veins

Varicose veins are blood vessels that fail to circulate blood properly and hence become swollen, bulging, and twisted due to blood pooling in the vein. They can sometimes be painful and reduce circulation around the body, but are mostly a cause for cosmetic concern as they cause unsightly discoloration and darkening of the skin. Varicose veins are more common in people who are overweight, pregnant, sedentary, or on their feet for long periods.
