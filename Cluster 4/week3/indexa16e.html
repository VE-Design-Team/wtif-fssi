Breaches and mandatory reporting

Mandatory reporting is a term used to describe the legislative requirement for selected groups of people (such as nurses, carers and teachers) to report suspected cases of elderly, disability or child abuse and neglect to government authorities. Parliaments in all Australian states and territories have formalised mandatory reporting laws, which can differ between states.

  • A breach is where a legal requirement, procedure or ethical requirement is not met. You must report any breaches to the appropriate person, usually your supervisor, as soon as reasonably possible.
  • According to legislation or your workplace policies and procedures, you must report all breaches to your supervisor, including signs of abuse, bullying, harassment or any unsafe or illegal practices.
  • Under the Aged Care Act, residential aged care providers must report any suspicions or allegations of assaults to the local police and the Department of Health. Reportable assaults include:
    • unlawful sexual contact with a resident: any sexual contact that is not consensual or involves a resident with a cognitive or mental impairment who cannot provide consent.
    • use of unreasonable force with residents: any deliberate, violent attacks on residents that causes visible harm, including punching, hitting or kicking.
  • Providers are also required to report any missing residents. A resident is missing if they are not in the facility and there is no reason for the absence.

Further resources

Most organisations will discuss mandatory reporting requirements at staff meetings and inform staff of requirements through policies, procedures, posters and training. You can help to make sure all staff in your workplace understand mandatory reporting requirements by helping with these activities.
