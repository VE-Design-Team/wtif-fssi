Advocacy services

Advocacy involves promoting or protecting the welfare and rights of an individual or group. The most common types of advocacy are self, family, individual, and systemic.

No matter what type of advocacy, people can get assistance from disability advocacy services. These are organisations that support people with a disability by upholding their rights through legal action and a complaints process. For individuals, this can involve identifying when discrimination occurs, informing them of their human rights, and communicating on their behalf with other people and organisations.

At a larger scale, disability advocacy services campaign for social change and will lobby governments when people with disabilities are treated unfairly and ensure their rights are recognised and met.

It is important to note that disability advocacy services do not provide mediation, supply counselling, deliver case management, nor will they make decisions on behalf of people with disabilities.

In Australia, the National Disability Advocacy Program (NDAP) is a national, government funded disability advocacy service. The NDAP provides a disability advocacy finder. The finder lists the locations and contact details of government funded disability advocacy services throughout Australia.

You can access the finder here: https://disabilityadvocacyfinder.dss.gov.au/disability/ndap/

Self-advocacy

A person with a disability represents themselves to uphold their own rights.

Group advocacy

Group advocacy is when a group of people come together who have similar attributes, beliefs or opinions and advocate on behalf of the group.

Individual advocacy

An individual advocate works one-on-one with a person with a disability to uphold their rights and resolve complaints or problems involving discrimination, abuse, or neglect.

They will develop an individual advocacy plan with the service user to determine how best to meet their goals.

Systematic advocacy

Systemic advocacy aims to enact long-term social change by influencing legislation, government policies and practices, and changing community attitudes to ensure the rights of people with disabilities are upheld and protected.

This involves educating the public so that they have a better understanding of the experiences and challenges people with disabilities face.
