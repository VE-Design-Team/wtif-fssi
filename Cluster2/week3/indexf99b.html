Identity development

The first theme affecting independence is identity development. This is being able to create stories about ourselves through our life experiences. These stories come from the decisions we make and tell us, and others, who we are. People with disability typically need help from their family or support services. Having these people may be important to helping them through each day, but it can also limit their opportunities to make meaningful decisions, interact with others to build social networks, and develop an understanding of themselves and what they are fully capable of. This is called ‘lack of voice’, when the service user feels excluded while others make choices for them.  

Enabling identity development means involving the service user in the decision-making process. They should have a say in what, when and how their support services are provided. Part of making decisions is allowing for dignity of risk. This is when an individual makes decisions about their life that others may not agree with or is contrary to their best interests.

An example is smoking. Engaging in risk-taking behaviour is part of growing up and discovering ourselves. People with disabilities are no different. They should be able to take risks, succeed or fail, and learn from their experiences. Regardless of whether you agree with the choices they make, your role as a support worker is to encourage them to make informed decisions and provide them with the necessary information and resources.
