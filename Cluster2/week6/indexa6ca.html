Abuse

In Australia, people with disability experience significant disadvantages and poorer health outcomes than the general population, making them some of the most vulnerable people in our society. The Royal Commission into Family Violence have indicated that women with a disability are 40 percent more likely to be victims of domestic violence and that 90 percent of women with intellectual disabilities have been sexually abused.

There are two effects of disability that put people at higher risk for physical and sexual abuse and assault: direct and socially mediated effects.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about the two effects of disability that increase the risk of abuse and assault.

Direct Effects

Direct effects refer to biological characteristics of a disability that negatively impact the personâ€™s ability to read or understand a situation and how other people respond to them. People with intellectual, developmental, and/or psychiatric disabilities are, at times, less able to identify danger or know when to take precautions, how to defend themselves, or know when and how to get help.

People with disabilities may display certain behaviours or have difficulty communicating with others that lead to misinterpretations of actions and intentions. For example, a police officer could misread how a person with a psychiatric disability is acting as being aggressive or threatening and respond with physical force to get them under control. A lack of communication would make the situation worse, especially if the person is on their own and needs a family member or support worker to help them translate.

Socially-mediated Effects

Socially-mediated effects refer to how people with disabilities are viewed, treated, and educated in society.

In general, people with disabilities have lower levels of education and may be treated like children well into adulthood. They are more likely to be denied sex education and opportunities to develop age-appropriate social skills. This can make them more vulnerable to abuse, especially when they are taught to trust and treat anyone providing support in the same way as their family. They are told to be compliant, rather than assertive.

For people with disabilities who are reliant on support workers for care, power inequalities can develop. People can become trapped in risky situations with unsafe support workers or carers if there are limited resources and they have no other options. The opposite can also be problematic when people with disabilities are exposed to multiple support workers over a short period of time. This can increase their chance of experiencing abuse.
