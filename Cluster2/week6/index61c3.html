Safe environments

People with disabilities tend to be more vulnerable and susceptible to abuse because they may not fully understand what safety is or how to protect themselves.

For example, a person with an intellectual disability may have difficulty judging who to trust. Everyday precautions, like keeping their front door locked or staying off the road, may also not be obvious to them. Differentiating between safe and dangerous environments can be a challenge.

Being safe doesnâ€™t just mean being free from harm. It also means feeling safe and in control in situations. For those who have ever felt powerless, abused, or neglected, rebuilding a sense of safety will take time and involve addressing multiple factors.

A safe space is where a person feels comfortable and secure. It is a place where they can be themselves and build their confidence and wellbeing.

A safe space doesnâ€™t necessarily have to be a physical space. It can also be whenever they are with supportive family or friends who make them feel fully accepted and loved.

A network of friends is a valuable source of support and advice. Good relationships involve give and take with the best intentions and knowing you can rely on each other.

Being a friend to others can give a person with a disability a sense of purpose and worth. This is important for improving self-esteem, developing their identity, and becoming more resilient.

Helping people with disabilities develop a sense of power and value requires communication. Providing relationship-focused support by emphasising the importance of talking and listening will encourage them to communicate the good and bad in their lives.

When they feel comfortable talking about their feelings, they are more likely to share moments when they feel unhappy or unsafe. Knowing when they feel vulnerable can lead to prevention and intervention to resolve a negative situation.

While avoiding unfamiliar people might be one tactic to staying safe, it can also lead to social isolation. Meeting and interacting with different people is an essential part of life and has many positive benefits for a personâ€™s wellbeing.

As a support worker, it is important to maintain regular and consistent contact with the service user and their family and carers. Not only will this help you identify and minimise behaviours or actions that cause the service user stress, it will inform you as to when you may need to intervene and help them manage or resolve a negative situation and its effects.

When entering a new environment, having some form of trusted support that provides guidance and reassurance to a person with a disability can make an unknown situation safer.

For example, a person with a disability is starting a new school. Having a familiar support worker attend the first few classes with them, until they are comfortable with the new teaching and learning staff, would make the transition easier.

Building a safe environment requires clear communication. Establish expectations and boundaries of everyone involved in the service userâ€™s support circle to create and maintain good, solid, long-term relationships.

Education is key to helping people with disabilities learn what safety is, the type of protective behaviours they should practice, and what they can do in situations they feel unsafe in.

Lessons and discussions should be tailored to the individual according to their needs and abilities. Being able to understand and apply the knowledge and skills they are taught will foster self-confidence and further independence.
