Positive reinforcement

Positive reinforcement is a strategy for addressing behaviours of concern. Behaviours of concern are behaviours that a person with disability may exhibit, which causes harm to themselves and others, restricts their ability to access the community, negatively impact a person’s quality of life, and pose safety concerns for themselves and the people around them.

These behaviours are usually an indication that something is wrong. Looking beyond the behaviour itself to understand why the person is acting this way is crucial to finding a way to reduce and stop it. Positive reinforcement is about identifying the issues to behaviours of concern, finding more appropriate responses for the person, and helping them apply these alternatives by rewarding their efforts until the new behaviour becomes routine.

Select each of the check boxes to learn how to establish a positive reinforcement strategy.
  • Identify the behaviour of concern, how often it occurs and under what circumstances.
  • Understand why the person presents the behaviour of concern. Think about when it happens and speak with the service user and those closest to them about it. Are there certain triggers that lead to the behaviour? Is the person acting this way because they are trying to get, avoid or escape something?
  • Once you fully understand the reason behind the behaviour of concern, identify better replacement behaviours that meet the same purpose and need of the service user.
  • Determine how to positively reinforce the new replacement behaviour. Have a discussion with the service user, their carers and family about their likes, dislikes and motivators. Decide on what type of positive reinforcement to give, when to give it and how often.
  • Teach the new replacement behaviour to the service user by showing them.
  • Whenever the person demonstrates the replacement behaviour, immediately reward them with the positive reinforcement. You need to be consistent, especially at the start when they are learning to adopt the replacement behaviour.
  • Once the replacement behaviour has become more regular, you can begin to fade out the positive reinforcement. Also consider whether this behaviour can be generalised to different situations that the person may face. If so, figure out what these other environments might be and encourage the person to practice the replacement behaviour in those instances.
