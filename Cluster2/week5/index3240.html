Stress management

Stress is the tension we feel under difficult circumstances. It can affect us physically, emotionally and mentally. Stress can be positive, giving us the energy to take on challenges, but if experienced long-term, it can negatively impact our well-being. Stress management is about finding a balance. We all experience stress in our day-to-day lives and it is important to know how to stop it from overpowering us. This means helping service users identify when they are stressed, what is the cause of it, and what they can do to change the situation for the better. A sign that someone is under stress is when there is a change in their behaviour that is inconsistent with their normal manner.

Ask them how they feel. Help them to recognise the signs of stress. Do they clutch their hands? Do they suddenly feel very warm and have trouble focusing on what people are saying? Teach them how to relax. This is best to do when the person is not stressed so they can develop these relaxation methods to prevent and manage their stress, not just react to it. When teaching stress management techniques, make sure they have everything they need to practice it readily available, and ensure the person feels comfortable with it, notices a positive improvement when employing the technique, and has alternatives if one method isnâ€™t working. People with disabilities may need extra support or intervention. Be prepared by creating a plan with them about what to do in the event of a stressful situation.

Deep breathing

Ask the service user to sit, stand or lie down in a comfortable position and close their eyes. Get them to take deep slow breaths, counting to five for each in-breath and each out-breath.

They can also visualise this process by placing a hand on their chest to feel it rise and fall, by raising and lowering an arm, or using their hand to gesture in and out with each breath.

Visual meditation

Have the service user focus on a photo, video, or object that they find calming. Ask them to tell you about it. For example, if it is photo of their family at the beach, have them describe the outing. What was the weather and scenery like?

What was the feel of the sand and water, or the sound of the waves and birds? Make this a routine that whenever theyâ€™re feeling stressed, they concentrate on this photo, video or object and talk about all the positive thoughts and memories it brings to mind.

Long walks

If the person is feeling anxious and unable to sit still to relax, go for a walk with them. Encourage them to take deep breaths while walking. Explain to them that walking is a good way to shake off nervous energy. Being outside and seeing other things and people as they go can also help distract and calm them down.

Aquatics

Some people find that being in the water, where they can fully stretch out, is a good way to release tension. For people with disabilities that have difficulty getting in and out of a pool, there is equipment that can lower and raise them in and out of the water. Check the facilities to make sure this is available.

Music

Listening to music is different to having music playing in the background. Have the service user stop what theyâ€™re doing and focus on the music. Try various types of music to see which they respond most positively to. The purpose of the music is to divert their attention away from their worries to what they are hearing.

Relaxed posture

When stressed, people tend to adopt positions that commonly strain their neck, shoulders and back. A relaxed position will help ease some of that physical tension in the body. To sit with a relaxed posture, make sure the person can place both feet flat on the ground with their knees at a 90Ëš angle, their back and shoulders are straight, and their head is upright facing forward.

This position can be modified depending on the personâ€™s disability. Be sure to sit with them to demonstrate how to sit properly. You can also pair this with some other stress management techniques like deep breathing, visual mediation, listening to music and progressive muscle relaxation.

Progressive muscle relaxation

This involves consciously tightening and relaxing different muscles in the body, starting from the toes and moving up to the head. When explaining to and directing the individual, be sure to only include muscles and body parts they recognise and understand.

This technique is not recommended for people with muscle spasticity.
