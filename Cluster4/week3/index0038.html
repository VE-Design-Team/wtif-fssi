Signs of abuse and neglect

Service users living with impaired cognitive or physical function are especially vulnerable to abuse and neglect. As an aged care or disability support worker, you are in a good position to identify changes that may suggest a service user is being abused or neglected. Be observant and aware. Abuse can be physical, psychological, emotional, sexual, and financial. Neglect is the failure to meet the needs of the service user.Â 

Click on the boxes below to reveal possible signs and symptoms of abuse and neglect.
  • A service user being in poor health when it is not caused by an illness.
  • Untreated ailments and injuries.
  • Personal hygiene needs that have not been attended to (e.g. toileting).
  • Unsanitary and unclean living conditions (e.g. dirty room, soiled bedding, faecal/urine smell, inadequate clothing).
  • Injuries including bruises, cuts and burns that do not have an obvious explanation.
  • Unexplained disappearance of belongings.
  • Forced isolation (e.g. locking a service user in their room because of challenging behaviour).
  • Inappropriate administration of medication (e.g. sedating a service user unnecessarily).
  • Rushing a service user to meet staff/organisation schedules.
  • Depriving a service user of their cultural or religious rights.
  • Indifferent, angry, aggressive or flirtatious behaviour towards a service user.

Further resources

Most organisations will discuss mandatory reporting requirements at staff meetings and inform staff of requirements through policies, procedures, posters and training. You can help to make sure all staff in your workplace understand mandatory reporting requirements by helping with these activities.
