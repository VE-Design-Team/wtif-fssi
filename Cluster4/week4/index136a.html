Parts of the cardiovascular system

Blood vessels are tubes that carry blood to and from the heart, distributing it to every area of the body. The volume of blood it is required to carry dictates the size of a blood vessel. Two of the main blood vessels in the body are the aorta and vena cava. There are three major types of blood vessels: arteries, veins, and capillaries. Blood flows away from the heart through arteries, which gradually branch and narrow into tiny capillaries in which nutrients and waste are exchanged. These capillaries then widen and join to become veins, which are used to return blood to the heart.

  • Arteries: Represented as red in this diagram, arteries are blood vessels that carry blood away from the heart. Blood leaving the heart is expelled with a great amount of force or pressure; therefore, arteries have thicker, more muscular walls than other blood vessels to withstand this pressure. The largest artery in the body is the aorta, which extends from the heart down to the abdomen and distributes oxygenated blood to all parts of the body.
  • Heart: The heart is the primary organ in the cardiovascular system, pumping over five litres of blood through the body every minute. It has two sides and four chambers. Each side has a smaller upper chamber at the top called an atrium, and a larger lower chamber at the bottom called a ventricle. Blood enters the heart through the atria and exits through the ventricles. When the heart is pumping, its chambers are said to be in one of two states: systole, when the heart muscles are contracting to push blood out of a chamber; and diastole, when the muscles are relaxed to allow blood into the chamber. When measuring a personâ€™s blood pressure, we talk about systolic and diastolic pressure: these are the rates at which blood is being pushed out of, and flowing into, the heart. In a blood pressure reading of 120/80, for instance, the systolic pressure (ventricular contraction) is the top number, and the diastolic pressure (ventricular relaxation).
  • Veins: Represented as blue in this diagram, veins are blood vessels that return blood to the heart from other areas of the body. Unlike arteries, they deal mainly with quite low blood pressures, meaning they can be thinner and less elastic than arteries. The two vena cavae are the largest veins in the human body, transporting blood to the heart from the legs, head and arms.
