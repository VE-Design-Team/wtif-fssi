Common issues with the endocrine system

Issues in the endocrine system relate to specific glands within the system. If a gland is not working properly, it will over- or under-produce a particular hormone, leading to problems in the area that that gland controls.

The following are some common disorders and diseases that can affect the endocrine system.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about some of the common issues with this system.

Diabetes

Diabetes is a condition of the pancreas, in which the body cannot produce enough insulin to maintain healthy levels of glucose in the blood. There are three types of diabetes:

  • Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system prevents proper insulin production.
  • Type 2 diabetes is related to lifestyle factors such as obesity and poor diet.
  • Gestational diabetes occurs during pregnancy and, though it can cause birth complications if left untreated, is usually temporary.

Symptoms of diabetes include thirst, fatigue, slow-healing wounds, itching, weight loss, mood swings, passing more urine. Failure to regulate the disease can lead to serious complications along with damage to the kidneys, eyes, heart, feet, and nerves.

Hypothyroidism

Hypothyroidism is the result of an underactive thyroid gland. It occurs mainly in women, affecting up to 25% of women over the age of 65 years. Symptoms include unexplained fatigue and weight gain, depression, low heart rate, puffy face, hair loss and goitre.

Premenstrual Syndrome (PMS)

PMS is a common problem related to hormonal fluctuations, that affects girls and women who are menstruating. Some physical and mood changes are normal as part of the menstrual cycle; however, when these changes occur regularly, and become intense enough to affect daily function, they are referred to as PMS. PMS can make females feel anxious, depressed, and irritable. It can also have physical affects related to breast tenderness, bloating, fatigue, and acne outbreaks.

Disrupted Sleep Patterns

Human sleeping and waking patterns are called ‘circadian rhythms’. Disruption to a person’s circadian rhythm can be related to hormonal imbalances, especially imbalances involving the pineal gland. Sleep disorders like insomnia can be a sign that the pineal gland is not producing enough melatonin. Disruptions to a person’s circadian rhythms may be due to biological or hormonal factors, such as old age or puberty. They can also be a result of external factors such as shift work or jet lag. Long-term disturbances of a person’s circadian rhythms are linked to cancer, hypertension, heart disease, diabetes, and mental health issues like depression and rheumatoid arthritis.
