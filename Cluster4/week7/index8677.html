Integumentary system

The integumentary system gets its name from the word ‘integument’, which refers to an organism’s natural covering, like a skin, husk, shell or rind. In humans, the integumentary system acts as a barrier between the body and the outside world, protecting our tissues and organs from disease, light, foreign matter, and physical damage. It also retains fluids within the body, regulates body temperature, eliminates waste products and serves as a receptor for touch, pain, pressure, heat and cold.

Finally, it stores water, fat, and vitamin D. As it is such a large system that covers a considerable area of the body, the integumentary system works closely with many other systems and is involved in many physiological processes. This system has the largest organ in the human body, your skin. Other organs include glands, nails and hair.

Parts of the integumentary system

Select the + hotspots to learn more about the different parts that make up this system.
  • Skin: Skin is the largest organ in the body. It has three layers: the epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis. The epidermis is the outer, waterproof layer of skin, consisting of around 45 rows of stacked cells. This layer contains melanin, which gives our skin colour, receives sensations for touch etc., and contains cells that allow the immune system to fight foreign bodies. Cells in lower rows of the epidermis are gradually pushed to the topmost layer where they die and are shed. The dermis is the middle layer of skin. It is comprised of connective tissue that gives skin strength and elasticity. It also houses the hair follicles, lymph vessels, sweat and oil glands, and the ends of blood vessels and nerves. The hypodermis is a layer of subcutaneous tissue that connects the skin to underlying muscles and bones. It contains fatty tissue that stores energy and helps insulate the body and protect internal organs.
  • Hair: Hair protects the body from UV radiation. It also provides insulation by trapping warm air close to the skin. Head hair grows at different rates in different people, but the average is around 15 centimetres per year. As a person ages, pigment cells in the hair follicle die, and the hair turns grey.
  • Skin Glands: This skin contains glands that help protect the body and allow it to regulate its temperature. Sweat glands are found in almost every part of the skin, and secrete substances that work to lower body temperature and cool skin through evaporation. Sebaceous glands produce oils that waterproof skin and promote elasticity. Ceruminous glands are found only in the ear canals. They produce wax to lubricate the eardrum and prevent foreign matter entering the ear canal.
  • Nails: Nails are organs that protect the ends of the fingers and toes from soft-tissue injury. They are also used for scraping, sensation and fine motor-control of objects. Nails are made of protein and grow from deep in the epidermal layer to push through the surface of the skin. They are permeable and can absorb water as well as harmful chemicals found in items such as cosmetics and cleaning products.
