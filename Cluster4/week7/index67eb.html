Lymphatic system

The lymphatic system is a network of delicate tubes, nodes and organs that works closely with both the circulatory system and the immune system. In conjunction with the circulatory system, it carries fluids, nutrients and waste between the blood and tissues, and in conjunction with the immune system, it filters bacteria, viruses and foreign substances that may cause disease.

The main job of the lymphatic system is to transport lymph fluid from the tissues to the bloodstream, filtering and cleaning it of debris and pathogens. Lymph fluid is a thin, colourless fluid made mainly of white blood cells.

Parts of the lymphatic system

Select the + to learn more about the different parts that make up this system.
  • Tonsils: The tonsils are the largest lymph nodes in the body, comprising two soft masses of tissue at the rear of the pharynx. They are the bodyâ€™s first line of defence against bacteria or foreign substances that are ingested or inhaled; with specialised cells on their surface to combat infection. If they become overly inflamed, they can develop tonsillitis or block the flow of air to the throat.
  • Thymus Gland: The thymus gland transforms white blood cells into T cells, and releases these cells into nearby lymph nodes. T cells work with the immune system to fight infection and disease. The thymus gland is at its largest during childhood, but shrinks from puberty onward to become a small lump of fatty tissue. (See Immune system for more information.)
  • Vena Cava: The main vein in the human body is where the lymph fluid enters the blood stream again at the end of its journey through the lymphatic system.
  • Spleen: The spleen filters and stores blood. It is made up of two types of tissue: red pulp and white pulp. Red pulp tissue filters the blood and removes faulty red blood cells. White pulp contains white T and B cells that help fight infection. In the case of rapid blood loss, the spleen functions as a source of stored blood. Like the gall bladder, the spleen is not a vital organ, and can be removed if damaged or defective. Other organs will then step in to perform its function.
  • Lymphatic Vessels: Lymph nodes are collection centres for lymph, found most commonly around the throat, neck, chest, armpits and groin. They are bundles of tissue that contain large numbers of B and T cells, designed to filter lymphatic fluid and remove any bacteria, pathogens or damaging molecules. The presence of an infection or damaging pathogen can cause an influx of antigens to the lymph node, causing it to swell and increase in size. As such, swollen lymph nodes are often a visual symptom of an infection.
