Urinary system

Select the + to learn more about the different parts that make up the respiratory system.
  • Kidneys: Humans have two kidneys, one on either side of the body. In the urinary system, the kidneys’ main functions is to remove toxins, waste, and excess water from the blood, collecting and expelling these products from the body as urine. Clean, filtered blood is then returned to be recirculated around the body, while urine continues through the urinary system via the ureters and into the bladder. The body’s blood supply circulates through the kidneys roughly every five minutes. The kidneys also play a part other body systems by maintaining blood pressure and regulating certain hormones.
  • Ureters: The ureters are long narrow tubes only a few millimetres wide. They link the kidneys to the bladder, and can stretch to accommodate varying volumes of urine as it passes through them. Urine is moved through the ureters using peristalsis, so that waste can continue to be removed from the kidneys even while we are lying down.
  • Bladder: The bladder is the storage tank of the urinary system. It is an elastic sac lined with tiny wrinkles that allow it to expand in order to delay and control the frequency of urination. The bladder also controls strong muscles designed to keep urine in when required, and force it out when needed. By collecting urine, the bladder allows us to urinate once every few hours or so, rather than constantly leaking urine throughout the day.
  • Urethra: The urethra is a tube that transports urine stored in the bladder and expels it from the body. The length of the urethra varies between sexes, being around 4cm long in females, and around 20cm long in males. In males it extends the full length of the penis, as well as eliminating urine from the body, functions as part of the reproductive system to transport semen during ejaculation.
