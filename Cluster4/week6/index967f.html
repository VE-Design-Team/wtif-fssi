Female reproductive system

The reproductive system is the only body system not vital to life or human function. However, without it humans would be unable to procreate, give birth, and pass on genetic material to future generations. The female reproductive system has several functions:

  • to produce and transport ova required for fertilisation
  • to produce hormones that maintain the reproductive cycle
  • to support the developing foetus during pregnancy
  • to facilitate lactation to feed an infant.

Parts of the female reproductive system

Select the + to learn more about the different parts that make up this system.
  • Fallopian Tubes: The fallopian tubes provide a pathway between the ovaries and the uterus. They are covered with tiny projections and hairs that pick up ova at one end, and carry them along to the uterus. The fallopian tubes are where conception usually occurs; that is, where the sperm meets the ova and fertilisation takes place. The fertilised egg is then moved to the uterus.
  • Uterus: The uterus is also known as the ‘womb’. Once fertilisation has taken place, the fertilised egg is implanted in the wall of the uterus, where it will be nourished and develop over the next nine months. Unfertilised ova are eliminated through menstruation, which is the monthly shedding of the lining of the uterus. During childbirth, muscles in the uterus contract to push the foetus through the cervix and into the birth canal.
  • Ovaries: The ovaries are two small glands located on the left and right side of the pelvic region. They produce ova (eggs), which join with sperm to become a zygote, and eventually a foetus. They also produce the female sex hormones, estrogen and progesterone. Each month, the ovaries release one ovum which is transported via the fallopian tubes to the uterus.
  • Cervix: The cervix is a narrow canal between the uterus and vagina. It allows sperm to pass through during ovulation, but in pregnancy prevents external pathogens from entering the uterus and harming a developing foetus. During childbirth, the cervix dilates to around 10 centimetres to allow the head of the foetus to enter the birth canal.
  • Vagina: The vagina has several functions: it accommodates the penis during intercourse, it connects the cervix to the outside of the body providing a passage for birth, and it allows menstrual blood to exit the body. Like the cervix, the vagina stretches considerably to allow the foetus to pass through during childbirth.
  • Breasts: The breasts contain specialised glands, called mammary glands, designed to produce milk for a newborn infant through breastfeeding. The mammary glands are activated shortly following childbirth, and remain active as long as an infant continues to suckle. When an infant stops breastfeeding, the mammary glands ‘dry up’ and milk is no longer produced.

The menstrual cycle

The term ‘menstrual cycle’ refers to regular and cyclical changes in the female reproductive system that make pregnancy possible. Though it varies from woman to woman, the average menstrual cycle is 28 days and has four phases. It begins with menstruation, which is the elimination of the uterine lining through the vagina. In the second phase, hormones are released that stimulate the ovaries to produce ova. The third phase is ovulation, in which one mature egg is released into the fallopian tube and carried towards the uterus in the hope of meeting a sperm. The final phase causes the lining of the uterus to thicken in anticipation of receiving a fertilised egg. If fertilisation doesn’t occur, the thickened lining of the uterus falls away and the cycle begins again with menstruation.
