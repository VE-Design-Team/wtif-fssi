Healthy lifestyle: strategies

In residential aged care facilities, workers use several strategies to promote health and wellbeing.

Select each strategy to find out more about it.

Nutrition and Hydration

One of the most important factors of a healthy mind and body is water. Older people are particularly susceptible to dehydration. Dehydration has serious health consequences and can make the resident tired, cranky and stiff-jointed. Dehydration can cause nausea, headaches, cramps and more serious consequences, such as seizures, coma or death.

Exercise

Exercise helps to improve physical health and independence. Through exercise, residents can improve their balance, reducing the risk of falls. Exercise can also help reduce depression, anxiety and the progression of osteoporosis. Exercise works by:

  • improving sleep patterns
  • changing the levels of chemicals in the brain
  • interrupting negative thoughts
  • increasing coping ability
  • socialising with others.

When designing exercise activities for older people, it is important to consider their interests, motivations and physical abilities. Leading a group exercise activity requires specialised training. However, you could walk or do gentle exercise with a resident, provided they are capable.

Oral Health

Older people still require good oral hygiene and regular dental check-ups. As you age, the amount of saliva produced decreases. Saliva cleans the teeth and the reduction increases vulnerability to tooth decay and infections.

Some residents will need assistance brushing their teeth. If the resident has dentures, you must check these regularly to ensure they fit properly and are in good condition.

Relaxation

Several activities promote relaxation. Gardening is one activity that can promote relaxation and can be tailored to meet the individual needs of residents.

For example, raised garden beds and adequate chairs can help make gardening more accessible. Massage is another activity that promotes relaxation. Trained massage therapists can provide massage to help residents relax.

Social Activities

Social activities aim to promote connectedness. Social activities may include buddy programs or men’s sheds. Volunteers also provide a wide range of social programs, including sports, recreation, religious and educational programs.

Some residential aged care facilities use animal and pet therapy. This involves introducing a pet, such as a cat or dog, into the facility.

Reflection

In the later stages of life, many people take time to reflect on the life they have lived. Reminiscing can help residents to resolve conflicts from the past and develop appreciation for their life.

It can also be used to re-evaluate and develop coping strategies. While detailed reminiscence should only be undertaken with a qualified practitioner, simple storytelling or communicating with residents about their lives is something anyone can do.

This is particularly effective for residents with dementia. Prayer and spiritual counselling may be used to help residents reminisce. Only suitably qualified practitioners should undertake this type of activity.

Music and Arts

Music and art activities help to give residents a sense of purpose and offer sensory stimulation, as well as being relaxing and enjoyable. Dancing, singing, listening to music or playing instruments can help residents to relax and express their feelings. Craft groups and activities are also effective. These can include knitting, needlecraft, paper crafts, painting, flower arranging or woodwork.

Sensory Stimulation

Sensory stimulation helps to make the environment more comfortable and enjoyable, which, in turn, promotes health and wellbeing. Simple activities that can provide sensory stimulation include:

  • using bright colours
  • making gardens accessible
  • placing artwork on walls
  • using pillows, throws and rugs in lounge areas
  • playing music
  • providing manicures and hair care
  • providing interesting meals
  • playing movies
  • group games or activities
  • having fresh flowers in the facility.
