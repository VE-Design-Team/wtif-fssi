Protective factors

You can take steps that decrease the likelihood of a mental health disorder and decrease the potential severity of the consequences of the disorder. Protective factors are those that give people resilience and ‘protect’ them from the potential impacts and symptoms of a disorder.

Select each protective factor to find out more about it.

Access to services

Health professionals can assist residents by assessing, advising and providing therapy. Consulting health professionals early can reduce the severity and length of a mental health disorder. Health professionals, such as dieticians, counsellors, physiotherapists and occupational therapists, can help to prevent mental health disorders.

Autonomy

Older people generally want to keep their independence and autonomy. In order to do so, they must be able to participate in daily activities, make social connections and make decisions about their care. As a support worker, you can support the resident to be autonomous by:

  • doing things with them, rather than doing things to them or for them
  • acknowledging the resident’s role
  • learning more about the resident’s values and lifestyle
  • providing opportunities for the resident to practice and use their skills
  • encouraging the resident to learn new skills
  • asking the resident how they want to solve problems relating to their care
  • providing the resident with opportunities to be involved in activities that are valued, such as volunteer work or organising activities
  • encouraging the resident to self-manage their health.

Sense of purpose

Staying active and enjoying life has a positive impact on health and well-being. It is important that residents have a sense of purpose and feel productive. To ensure this, residents need to engage in meaningful activities.

Family and friends

Feeling supported by friends and family members can help residents to feel that they are still loved and important. Having regular visitors is an important part of feeling connected and wanted.

Safe and comfortable environments

A safe and comfortable environment is important for wellbeing. Residents need to have a sense of privacy, security and safety. The environment must fulfil the basic physical needs of the residents, as well as their sensory needs.

A safe and comfortable environment might include appropriate lighting, temperature control, access to sunshine and an environment free from violence and abuse.
