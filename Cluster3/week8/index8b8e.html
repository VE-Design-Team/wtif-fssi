Mental health and behaviours of concern

Mental health is a critical part of overall health and well-being. Mental health refers to a personâ€™s ability to manage daily life challenges and social interactions. Mental health can have a significant impact on physical health. It is important for you to be able to identify and manage any signs and behaviours of concern. Mental health issues can affect anyone. Some residents will have experienced mental illness throughout their lives.

Others may develop a mental health illness in their older years. Unfortunately, mental health issues are often overlooked in older people because they are thought to be symptoms and signs of ageing rather than an underlying issue. Symptoms may develop suddenly or gradually and in the case of gradual development, detection of the symptoms requires considered attention across time as well as clear communication and case note recording within teams.

Mental health disorders involve a range of intersecting factors. These may include stressful life events that combine with biological predispositions and psychological vulnerabilities.

Anxiety is a term that refers to a range of anxiety disorders that generally involve feelings of worry or stress. Anxiety disorders include general anxiety disorder, phobias, obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD), post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), panic disorder and social phobia. Each of these conditions has its own signs and symptoms, however, the following are some of the general symptoms:

  • being worried or anxious most of the time
  • difficulties in calming down
  • feeling overwhelmed or frightened
  • experiencing recurring thoughts that may seem silly to others
  • avoiding situations that cause anxiety
  • experiencing ongoing difficulties after a traumatic event
  • agitation.

Bipolar disorder is a mood disorder in which the person has extreme mood swings. The person may feel joyful and energetic and then drop into a severe depression. Signs and symptoms of bipolar disorder include:

  • manic episodes leading to:
    • lack of sleep
    • rapid speech
    • reckless behaviour paired with impaired judgement
    • aggression
    • irritability
    • withdrawal
    • lack of concentration
    • changes in appetite
    • anxiety.

Dementia describes a collection of symptoms that are caused by disorders affecting the brain. It is not one specific disease. Dementia affects thinking, behaviour and the ability to perform everyday tasks. Brain function is affected enough to interfere with the person’s normal social or working life. Signs and symptoms of dementia include:

  • verbal disruption
  • physical aggression
  • repetitive actions or questions
  • resistance to personal care
  • sexually inappropriate behaviour
  • refusal to accept services
  • problems associated with eating
  • socially inappropriate behaviour
  • wandering or intrusiveness
  • difficulty sleeping.
