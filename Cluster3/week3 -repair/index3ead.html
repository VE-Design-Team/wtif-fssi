What is dementia?

Dementia is caused by damage to brain cells. This damage affects the brain cells’ ability to communicate with each other. When brain cells cannot communicate normally, it affects the person’s thinking, behaviour and feelings. Dementia is not a single specific disease. Dementia is a term that describes a variety of symptoms linked to memory decline and thinking skills. It reduces a resident’s ability to perform everyday activities. Dementia is progressive, which means it will get worse over time.

The advancement of dementia, from early to final stages of the disease, varies from person to person. The type of dementia a person has will determine how quickly the disease progresses and the likely cause of death. Service users with dementia eventually become dependent on personal care assistants (PCAs) in most areas of daily living. In the initial phases of the illness, the emphasis should be on maximising activity and integration to maintain independence.

Later, the resident will require palliative care. Once diagnosed, residents should be engaged early in discussions about care, including their end-of-life care, while they can still make decisions. From diagnosis to end-of-life, the quality of life of a dementia resident and their family is incredibly important. Dementia is a major health problem in Australia.

It is overwhelming not only for the people who have it, but also for their caregivers, families and friends. Researchers around the world are working to develop more effective treatments for dementia, aiming to eventually find a cure.
