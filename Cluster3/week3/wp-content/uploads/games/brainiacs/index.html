Dementia

Dementia is a broad term that describes a set of symptoms. Many diseases that cause dementia are caused by proteins building up in brain nerve cells. Some types of dementia are caused by reduced blood flow to these cells. Both causes result in brain cell damage and cell death.

Nerve cell damage can cause various symptoms and behaviour changes, depending on the regions of the brain with damaged cells.

People with dementia find it increasingly difficult to function normally. Dementia is eventually fatal.

Frontal lobe

The frontal lobe is involved with personality, emotions, behaviour, speech, writing skills, problem solving and storing long term memories.

Symptoms of dementia in the frontal lobe include:

  • personality and behaviour changes including behaving inappropriately
  • difficulty processing information and making decisions
  • repetitive and compulsive behaviours
  • depression and/or anxiety
  • physical or verbal aggression.

Parietal lobe

The parietal lobe is involved with putting things in order (e.g. letters and numbers), spatial awareness and body control.

Symptoms of dementia in the parietal lobe include:

  • changes in the way the person sees, hears and interprets what is happening
  • difficulty recognising frequently used objects e.g. brushing their hair with their toothbrush
  • difficulty judging where they are in their environment, making them prone to falls.

Temporal lobe & hippocampus

The temporal lobe and hippocampus are involved with memory (including recognising faces), vision and language (including remembering words).

Symptoms of dementia in the temporal lobe and hippocampus include:

  • memory loss and disorientation
  • forgetfulness e.g. where the keys were placed
  • problems with language and speech
  • difficulty with factual knowledge.

Occipital lobe

This occipital lobe is involved with vision and interpreting information from our eyes.

Symptoms of dementia in the occipital lobe include:

  • difficulty seeing what and where things are
  • illusions and hallucinations
  • misidentification e.g. mistaking one person for another
  • misperceptions e.g. mistaking a shadow for a hole in the ground.

Cerebellum

The cerebellum is involved with language, attention, body control, movement, posture, and balance.

Symptoms of dementia in the cerebellum include:

  • lack of balance and coordination
  • slurred speech
  • abnormal eye movements
  • tremors
  • slower movements.
