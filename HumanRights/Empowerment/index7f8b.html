The three generations of human rights

Human rights have been developed over a long period of time and our ideas about them have changed and developed. The history of human rights has been divided into three separate eras. These divisions are known as the three generations of human rights.

The three generations of human rights do overlap and in many instances a right developed in one generation has lead to further rights developed in later generations.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about the three generations of human rights.

First Generation

First generation rights were designed to protect individuals. These are often called negative rights, because they are the right to not have something bad happen. For example, the right to not be abused, the right to not be tortured, the right to not be forced into slavery, etc.

First generation rights include so called ‘liberty’ rights. These are the most well-known rights such as the right to life, the right to vote, the right to peaceful assembly, the right to freedom of expression and the right to equality before the law. 

Second Generation

Second generation rights guarantee equality. These are positive rights, meaning they are rights to certain things. They include the right to work, the right to join trade unions, to social security, an adequate standard of living including adequate food, clothing and housing, the right to education, the right to health and so forth.

Second generation rights are also referred to as positive rights because a much more active role has to be taken by the state to ensure that these rights are fulfilled. These rights require more resources, making them a source of significant political debate and the laws around these rights are often weaker.

Third Generation

Third generation rights are a collective rights for people and groups. These rights emerged in the late 20th century in response to criticism that human rights focused too much on individuals and did not reflect group and community cultures and values.

Third generation rights are largely unofficial and have been difficult to write into laws. These rights are only just starting to be written into treaties and conventions, such as the Declaration on the Right to Development, 1986.
