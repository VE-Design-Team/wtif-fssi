History of human rights

The definition and recognition of human rights have developed over centuries in eastern and western cultures. From the earliest philosophers, from religion and from social theorists across the ages, the rights of all human beings have been featured in various ways.

These changes are documented in the many human rights instruments that have been created and passed into law during significant historical events.

Select a spot on the timeline to learn more about important human rights instruments in history.

539 BC

After the defeat of the Babylonian king, the Cyrus Cylinder was written, banning slavery. Photograph by Mike Peel

250 BC

With the emergence of Buddhism, the Edicts of Ashoka were written, banning religious discrimination in most of South Asia.

1215

To prevent civil war in England, the Magna Carta was written, protecting free men from imprisonment and punishment except in accordance with the law.

1689

Following the Glorious Revolution in 1688, the English Bill of Rights was written, guaranteeing basic civil rights for the people.

1776

When the North American British colonies separated from Great Britain, the United States Declaration of Independence was written, stating that all men are equal with certain inalienable rights.

1789

After the French Revolution, the Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen was written, declaring “Men are born and remain free and equal in rights… these rights being liberty, property, security and resistance to oppression”.

1945

At the end of World War II, the United Nations was created and began writing the International Bill of Rights to define universal human rights.
