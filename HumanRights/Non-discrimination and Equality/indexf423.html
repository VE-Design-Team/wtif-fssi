Protecting the rights of others

We all have a role to play in defending the human rights of others, particularly those who are vulnerable. Part of the role of a support worker is to act to eliminate discrimination and promote equality.

Service users should be encouraged to advocate for themselves. Many will have family or friends who can advocate for them. Fellow service users may also provide support for and advocate for each other.Â 

In some instances, you may be the only person helping to promote and protect the rights of a vulnerable person. It is particularly important in these circumstances to listen deeply to the person you are supporting, to help them advocate for themselves and, where necessary, to take up any concerns you have to senior staff.

Consider how to provide support in the following scenarios.

Question 1 of 2

John is a new resident at an aged care facility. He had been living with his adult child, Anne, who has Down Syndrome. John has been taking care of Anne since birth and her well-being is important to him. John is unhappy with Anne's new living arrangements and asks you to try to find out some more information about it from the service supporting Anne. As John's support worker, should you get involved?



Incorrect

Ignoring what is important is John is not supportive and may negatively impact John's well-being.

Correct

Providing support to help John continue taking care of Anne in some capacity would be beneficial to Johnâs health and well-being, especially since this a role he values and has been doing for a long time. Providing support may mean helping John receive and send out paperwork, finding someone appropriate who can help John fill out paperwork or having regularly scheduled visits with Anne.
