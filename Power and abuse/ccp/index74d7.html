Setting the agenda

An agenda is a plan or list of things to be acted upon. It is a tool used against people to ensure the powerful get their way.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about how the powerful set the agenda to ensure they get their way.

Decisions

One of the more subtle ways in which the powerful rule is not just by making decisions, but by setting the agenda that leads to decisions and non-decisions (i.e. making sure that a decision is never actually made even though it keeps getting discussed). It might be, for example, that at the broadest level a government or parliament refuses to allow issues of disability discrimination to be discussed, much less resolved, by simply keeping it out of the parliamentary decision-making process completely.

If the issue manages to get on the agenda, one way to get it off, at least temporarily, is to set up a committee to consider the issues and make recommendations. Imagine if the committee never meets, fails to reach agreement or fails to address the specific issue of concern in its report.

Who decides the agenda

At a lower level, it might be that a nursing home refuses to allow family, friends and residents to hold or attend meetings at which they might challenge management. Alternatively, in a house meeting, the agenda is decided only by the staff group, and residents’ and family and friends’ views are neither sought nor listened to.

Secretive face

At a local government level, it might be that the annual plan around streets and footpaths is not allowed to take into account disability access as a funding criterion. It might be that the Council stacks the decision-making body with people they know will not take disability into consideration when making decisions. Lukes (2007) refers to this as power’s ‘secretive face’.

Deceptive face

Lukes (2007) also talks about power’s ‘deceptive face’, where trickery and psychological methods are the primary tool in shifting values. For example, two support workers begin to label a client as a trouble maker who is always telling stories about others.

Gradually the other clients and workers begin to believe these stories and instead of seeing her as a victim of abuse, see her instead as the cause of the problem, making her life doubly miserable.
