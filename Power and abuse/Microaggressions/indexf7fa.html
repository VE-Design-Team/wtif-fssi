Microaggressions: power in everyday life

In our everyday lives, power is rarely exercised or experienced as something that is overwhelming. Rather, it often involves small interactions, which we often fail to pick up or see. It is only after a period of time when it dawns on us that our lives have become unpleasant, and we realise that we have been the victim of a lot of very small forms of aggression over a prolonged period of time—making us feel unhappy and unsafe.

Insults and dismissals

Microaggression is a term that has been coined to capture this. The term ‘microaggression’ was first used in the United States during the 1970s by Harvard professor Chester Pierce. He defined microaggressions as the ‘insults and dismissals’ experienced by any marginalised group at a day-to-day level. Psychologist Derald Wing Sue (2010 a,b,c) took the definition further, describing microaggressions as “brief, everyday exchanges that send denigrating messages to certain individuals because of their group membership”. She describes microaggressions as “the subtle insults, demeaning approaches or attitudes that are used to position one group as dominant against another”. Microaggressions are “not simply isolated incidents, but rather part of structural inequalities” (Oberlin Microaggressions 2013).

Preconceived stereotypes

Microaggressions are often based on preconceived stereotypes and beliefs that the group in question are lesser than you, for example, because of some physical characteristic, such as their age or a disability. While microaggressions might not seem as bad as other forms of abuse, they have a cumulative effect and are an insidious and ongoing form of abuse. People with disability in particular may have been subjected to microaggressions over a lifetime, leading to similar outcomes such as poor mental health and trauma from major forms of abuse. In addition, microaggressions can often grow over time, leading to more significant forms of verbal, physical and sexual abuse.
