More complex forms of power

Language, the way we position groups in relation to others and the way we order societies into discrete geographical settings all involve more complex and less observable forms of power. The French philosopher Michele Foucault spent many years examining and explaining how this works.

Objectifying

One way is through ‘objectifying’ a group by portraying members of that group as if they form another, almost non-human, collection of individuals who have no feelings or sensibilities. This is often reflected in our choice of language. We talk about a particular group as if they are different from us.

Across history we have seen examples where this has happened for slaves as well as for people with mental illness or disability. We might even give them a slang name. We assume they have bad motives or that they are associated with bad effects. Sometimes we just assume they are stupid or less than human.

In all these ways we assume they are somehow lesser than us and we reinforce this through the language we use, both when they are not in our company and especially when they are.

Separating

Another way is by separating a group. Once they are objectified and separated from others, power and authority are given to government-recognised professionals such as doctors, psychologists, support workers; who then work to ensure that the group is kept separate through the use of various techniques, including involuntary incarceration.

For example, in the past people with mental illness were separated from the community and locked in institutions based on the recommendation of a doctor or psychologist. Some were given brain surgery in an attempt to make them like the rest of us (a practice that still occurs for some people with epilepsy).

Subjectification

Subjectification is the third way power is exercised in a more complex way. Here, workers and professionals who hold power over the separated group subject it to routine forms of humiliation and domination—or worse. One example at the start of the 1900s was ‘eugenics’. The belief was that people with intellectual disability would weaken the gene pool if they had children.

This led to separate male and female wards in the institutions, but also the sterilisation of women with intellectual disabilities which went on in some places right up to the 1960s. This happens when the professionals hold values and beliefs that deny human rights.

Examples of this include situations in group homes where workers do not allow the adult residents to pursue activities they are legally allowed to do, perhaps because a parent has insisted, but also because of moral judgements by the support worker that are inconsistent with human rights.
