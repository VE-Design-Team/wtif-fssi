Privacy and abuse

Sometimes it is difficult to make the decision between protecting someone from abuse and protecting their privacy.

Select the images to learn more about how protecting privacy and preventing abuse can sometimes conflict with each other or use the arrows to move through the images.

Closed doors

In homes where a large number of people are being supported, there is a very difficult choice to be had between upholding resident’s privacy rights and making sure that they are free from abuse by keeping them in view.

Open doors mean people can peer in, depriving residents of their privacy. But open doors also make it more difficult for perpetrators to abuse them.

Restricting access to information

Restricting carer’s access to case files and medical records may seem like a good way to protect privacy.

But it is bound to generate a feeling of distrust and concern. This is especially so if the client is unable to speak or communicate effectively to explain how they acquired bruising or other injuries.

Potential for abuse

You work in a nursing home and notice a personal care worker keeps shutting or semi-closing the door when they visit a particular female resident. What do you do?

There is no easy solution here. Yes, there is the potential for abuse to occur, but it could also be to protect the resident’s privacy. Speaking with the resident and worker involved, and then passing on any information to your supervisor is the best option.

Privacy Act

A family member asks you for the medical records and case files of their mother who has dementia. They say they are worried because their mother’s health seems to have declined and she seems worried all the time. You check with your manager who says the Privacy Act means you should say no. What do you do?

You can let the family know you have spoken to your manager and she has said no because of the Privacy Act. The family can pursue further legal options, but you have completed the task correctly in your role as a support worker.
