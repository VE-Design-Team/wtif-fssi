Power and gender

Select the images to learn more about gender and its affect on power, or use the arrows to move through the images.

We know that women are overwhelmingly the victim/survivors of family violence. We also know that men are overwhelmingly the perpetrators.

Each week in Australia one woman is murdered by their current or former male partner (click here for facts). This is not just about individual men and women, but much broader sets of factors affecting men and women more generally.

Masculine identities play an important role here, as do feminine identities, which in turn are played out by large numbers of people.

Violence against women and children by men is only one of the ways in which the gendered power imbalance plays out. It is also evident in more crude ways, like the distribution of income and wealth, and even employment.

Men generally earn much more than women. They are more likely to be employed full time than women. They are more likely to be managers than women and they dominate the ranks of senior management and company boards.

These power imbalances seem to operate right across the age spectrum, even into old age. What is described as ‘elder abuse’ may be better understood as another expression of the gendered nature of power than age. As summarised by the Institute for Family Studies:

“The available evidence suggests that (the) prevalence (of elder abuse) varies across abuse types, with psychological and financial abuse being the most common types of abuse reported, although one study suggests that neglect could be as high as 20% among women in the older age group” (Australian Longitudinal Study on Women’s Health [ALSWH], 2014).

Older women are significantly more likely to be victims than older men, and most abuse is intergenerational (i.e. involving abuse of parents by adult children), with sons being perpetrators to a greater extent than daughters.

For some women, the experience in older age of family violence, including sexual assault, represents the continuation of a lifelong pattern of spousal abuse (Cramer & Brady, 2013; Mann et al., 2014; UNDESA, 2013).

Evidence on elder abuse occurring outside of a familial context (e.g. in care settings) is particularly sparse.
