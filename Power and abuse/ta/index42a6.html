Factors of power and abuse

There are several contributing factors which can lead to the abuse of power in your workplace. Being aware of what is causing the abuse is another important part of your role in stopping it.

Select each of the headings below to learn more about factors affecting abuse in your workplace.

Governing body 

Your team

What is the team you are part of? What are its values? What are its ethical systems? Are they shared? Do you spend time talking about these matters? A strong commitment to openness and disclosure is crucial to highly functioning teams in care settings.

Celebrate sharing information. Applaud those who raise concerns. Find measures that might show how well the team is operating in keeping clients happy and safe. Share them.

The organisation

Another factor is the organisation you work for. Does it have a clear set of values? Does it commit to a highly trained workforce? Does it welcome feedback and suggestions for improvement? How robust are its policies and procedures for dealing with complaints?

Does it welcome and seek the views of residents and their friends and families? Does it share feedback and also report back on improvements that are made because of this? Does it disclose cases of abuse in ways that protect privacy?

Management and leadership

The management and leadership of the organisation is the factor which is ultimately responsible for putting into effect the cultural and policy structures to make sure that abuse never occurs and is speedily dealt with when it does. Does the CEO act as a great role model for others? Do they champion the organisation’s values?

How representative is the management team? Does it have a strong representation of women? What about people with disability? Or those from diverse sexual orientations? How does it try to ensure the views of these groups feed into management decision-making? What priority does it give to safety?

Governing body

Finally, there is the board or governing body which ultimately has responsibility for setting the overall direction of the organisation. Again, how representative is it of the people being served? Do the backgrounds of board members reflect a broad range of perspectives, or is it just business acumen that seems to be valued?
