Motivational vs. coercive questioning

How you use different interviewing or questioning techniques can be the difference between getting a great outcome when supporting a service user or having a more challenging support experience.

Select the tab heading to learn more about these two types of questioning.
  • Coercive
  • Motivational

This is a method of questioning that tries to direct the person you are talking with (e.g. a service user) to answer your questions in a particular manner. The goal of coercive questioning is to get the other person to agree with and to arrive at an end result that you had already planned for.

For example, “You didn’t close that door, did you?”, followed by, “We know that you didn’t close the door, so just own up to it and we can move on.”

Think of the stereotypical police interview in movies and TV shows. These often use a coercive approach. This communication approach can be appropriate in some cases, for example, with a service user who has dementia or who is refusing food that has been agreed on in their individual plan.

Motivational techniques are very different to coercive techniques and are more inclusive. These techniques were originally developed by William Miller and Stephen Rollnick (Miller, 1983; Miller & Rollnick, 1991) for use in the alcohol and other drug (AOD) field. They are now used across the health and community development sectors, including aged care and disability support. The key is to overcome a service users ambivalence, or their not caring, about a situation or activity. You are likely already doing this through your use of other communication techniques. The key principles of this approach are:

  • The questioning style is generally quiet and elicits information from the client.
  • Direct persuasion or coercion is not an effective method for resolving a service users ambivalence.
  • Motivation to change comes from the service user, and is not imposed on them from outside forces (e.g. you).
  • Readiness to change is not a trait of the client, but occurs as a result of interpersonal interaction.
  • It is the service user’s task, not the questioner’s, to specify and resolve their own ambivalence.
  • The questioner guides the service user by helping the service user to examine and resolve their ambivalence.
  • The relationship resembles a partnership or collaboration.

More information on motivational interviewing is available from the Australian Department of Health by searching ‘motivational interviewing’.
