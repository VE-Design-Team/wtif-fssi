Leisure and relationships outcomes for people using technology

The use of AT and social media can expand networks and relationships; leading to meaningful engagement for all people. Older people and people with disability are no different.

Select each of the headings to learn more about leisure and relationships outcomes for people with disability and older people using technology.

Sharing interests

Online engagement can help people stay connected with their interests and with others who share similar interests.

People can keep up-to-date on whatâ€™s happening in the world, explore new interests and meet new people. This ultimately contributes to their sense of identity and belonging.

Being connected can reduce feelings of isolation and loneliness by adding meaning to people’s lives and improving our overall well-being and quality of life.

Social engagement

Email and social media help people develop and maintain friendships, engage in networking and explore online purchasing.

These forms of communication can strengthen relationships between friends and family, engage people with memories and increase a sense of community.

Sense of purpose

An improved sense of self and purpose through online engagement can lead to a reduction in challenging behaviours. Challenging behaviours are often a result of people feeling unfilled in their lives due to a lack of relationships, stimulation or engagement.

Alarms and alerts

IT devices that link to alarms and alerts in home care settings can also support independent living.

People can communicate with their support worker through Telehealth and video-conferencing in their own homes or in aged care facilities. This allows them to maintain their health while saving money and removing the inconvenience of visiting health care professionals.

Relationships

Computers and IT have had numerous, significant positive outcomes for older people and people with disability.

Social networking reduces loneliness, with the use of Facebook promoting a sense of identity and well-being. Relationship apps can help individuals find love, happiness and feelings of connectedness by giving people greater meaning in their lives. This can lead to better relationships not only with the outside world, but also between support workers and users.

People with dementia engaging with social networks have shown improvements in cognition, behaviour, mood, relationships, new skills, and physical, mental and social stimulation.
